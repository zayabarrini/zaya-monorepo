# Kierkegaard's timeline

---

Of course. Here is a timeline of Søren Kierkegaard's life and posthumous influence, highlighting key events, publications, and relationships.

### **Søren Kierkegaard (1813-1855) – Timeline**

---

#### **Early Life & Education (1813-1840)**

- **1813:** Born on **May 5** in Copenhagen, Denmark. The youngest of seven children to Michael Pedersen Kierkegaard, a wealthy, deeply melancholic, and devout merchant.
- **1830:** Enters the University of Copenhagen to study theology, but immerses himself in philosophy and literature.
- **1834-1836:** Death of his mother and, shortly after, **three of his siblings**. His father reveals a profound sense of guilt, believing God's curse is upon the family. This "great earthquake" deeply marks Kierkegaard.
- **1837:** Meets **Regine Olsen** (14 years old). Begins working as a teacher.
- **1838:** His **father dies**. Publishes his first book, _From the Papers of One Still Living_.
- **1840:** Proposes to **Regine Olsen** (now 18). Passes his theological examination.

---

#### **The Authorship Begins: The Aesthetic & Ethical (1841-1846)**

- **1841:** **Breaks his engagement with Regine Olsen**, an event that catalyzes his writing. Publishes his university thesis, _On the Concept of Irony_. Travels to Berlin, where he hears Schelling's lectures.
- **1843:** His "annus mirabilis" (miracle year). Publishes a torrent of major works under various pseudonyms, exploring different "stages on life's way":
  - _**Either/Or**_ (Victor Eremita) – The aesthetic vs. the ethical.
  - _**Fear and Trembling**_ (Johannes de Silentio) – The "teleological suspension of the ethical" and the story of Abraham and Isaac.
  - _**Repetition**_ (Constantin Constantius).
  - Publishes _Three Upbuilding Discourses_ under his own name, beginning his parallel "edifying" works.

- **1844:** Publishes _**The Concept of Anxiety**_ (Vigilius Haufniensis) and _**Philosophical Fragments**_ (Johannes Climacus).
- **1845:** Publishes _**Stages on Life's Way**_.
- **1846:** Publishes _**Concluding Unscientific Postscript to Philosophical Fragments**_ (Johannes Climacus). This massive work is intended as the culmination of his pseudonymous authorship, critiquing Hegelian system-building and arguing that "truth is subjectivity." **The Corsair Affair** begins—a satirical newspaper mocks him, making him a figure of public ridicule.

---

#### **The "Religious Author" & The Attack on Christendom (1847-1855)**

- **1847:** Publishes _**Works of Love**_, a foundational text on Christian ethics.
- **1848:** A year of intense productivity and spiritual crisis. Writes _The Point of View for My Work as an Author_ (published posthumously).
- **1849:** Publishes _**The Sickness Unto Death**_ (Anti-Climacus), on despair and the self. Publishes _**Practice in Christianity**_ (Anti-Climacus), which presents a radical picture of Christ as the "offense" and critiques the established church.
- **1850-1854:** Continues to publish direct religious discourses. His public conflict with the State Church intensifies.
- **1854-1855:** **The Attack on the Church.** After the death of the powerful Bishop Mynster, his successor calls Mynster a "truth-witness." Kierkegaard erupts, publishing a series of polemical pamphlets in a newspaper, attacking the institutional church for watering down Christianity and making a comfortable living for its clergy. This is his all-out public campaign, **"The Moment"** (Øjeblikket).
- **1855:** **October 2** – Collapses on the street. Enters the hospital.
- **1855:** **November 11** – Dies at Frederik's Hospital, **at age 42**. His funeral becomes a site of public controversy. He is buried in the family plot.

---

#### **Posthumous Influence & Legacy**

- **1860s-1930s:** Initially influential in Scandinavia and German-speaking world (e.g., Ibsen, Nietzsche). Often misinterpreted as a proto-existentialist _atheist_.
- **1940s-Present:** **The "Kierkegaard Boom."** Rediscovered as the **"Father of Existentialism"** and a profound Christian thinker. Major translations into English and French. Influences thinkers like **Karl Barth** (neo-orthodoxy), **Martin Heidegger, Jean-Paul Sartre, Simone de Beauvoir, Albert Camus, Martin Buber**, and later **Postmodernists**.
- **Today:** Recognized as a seminal figure in philosophy, theology, psychology, and literary theory for his analyses of anxiety, despair, individuality, faith, and the human condition.

# The causes of death for his family and for himself

---

Certainly. Kierkegaard's personal life was marked by profound loss, which deeply shaped his philosophy of anxiety, guilt, and mortality. Here’s a breakdown of the causes of death for his immediate family and for himself.

### **Deaths in Kierkegaard's Immediate Family**

His father, **Michael Pedersen Kierkegaard** (1756–1838), outlived almost all of his children. The family mortality was exceptionally high, even for the era, and was interpreted by Søren as a sign of a divine "curse."

1.  **His Mother, Ane Sørensdatter Lund** (1768–1834): Died on **July 31, 1834**, at age 66. The likely cause was **cholera**, which was epidemic in Copenhagen at the time.
2.  **His Siblings (Five of six died before 1838):**
    - **Maren Kirstine** (1797–1822): Died at 25. Cause unknown, but likely **tuberculosis** or complications from childbirth.
    - **Nicoline Christine** (1799–1832): Died at 33. Cause unknown.
    - **Petrea Severine** (1801–1834): Died at 33, just weeks after their mother. Likely **typhus** or **cholera**.
    - **Peter Christian** (1805–1888): The only sibling to outlive Søren. He later became a bishop and struggled with mental illness.
    - **Niels Andreas** (1809–1833): Died at 24 in **Paterson, New Jersey, USA**. He had emigrated in despair. The official cause was **"pleurisy"** (often a term for tuberculosis).
    - **Michael Pedersen Kierkegaard Jr.** (1807–1819): Died in a **schoolyard accident** at age 12.

**The "Earthquake" or "Curse":** In 1835, shortly after the deaths of his mother and sister Petrea, Kierkegaard's father confessed that as a poor, cold, and desperate shepherd boy on the Jutland heath, he had once stood on a hill, cursed God, and felt thereafter that he was doomed. He also believed his sexual indiscretion with Søren's mother (impregnating her while a servant in his house shortly after his first wife's death) was a great sin. The rapid succession of deaths of his children seemed to confirm this divine punishment. This trauma fundamentally shaped Søren's concept of inherited sin, anxiety, and the "sickness unto death."

### **Søren Kierkegaard's Own Death (November 11, 1855)**

His death was the culmination of a lifelong physical and psychological struggle.

- **Immediate Cause & Illness:** He collapsed in the street on **October 2, 1855**, and was taken to **Frederik's Hospital**. The primary cause of death was recorded as a **"tuberculous infection of the lungs"**—essentially, **tuberculosis**.
- **Underlying & Contributing Factors:**
  1.  **Physical Debilitation:** He had suffered from various ailments his whole life, including **severe spinal curvature** (likely kyphosis or scoliosis), which caused him a slight hunchback and chronic pain. He also had a **slight paralysis** from a childhood fall.
  2.  **Psychological Exhaustion:** The final, furious public attack on the Danish State Church (**"The Moment"** pamphlets in 1854-55) consumed all his energy. He saw it as his religious duty and published until his physical collapse.
  3.  **Refusal of Treatment:** In the hospital, he largely refused medicine and pastoral visits from the official church clergy he was critiquing. He saw his death as part of his witness.

- **Circumstances of Death:** He died peacefully in the hospital. His friend, the young theologian **Emil Boesen**, visited him frequently and recorded their profound final conversations. Kierkegaard's last recorded words were, **"Hold fast to Christ."**
- **Controversial Funeral:** His death and burial became a final act of protest. His nephew Henrik Lund caused a scene at the graveside, protesting the church's role in the ceremony. Kierkegaard had wanted a simple, unadorned burial, but his brother Peter Christian, now a bishop, arranged a church service. This dissonance perfectly encapsulated the conflict Kierkegaard had spent his final years fighting.

# How would Sade's (death 1814) writings transform Kierkegaard (Born in 1813)

---

This is a fascinating and provocative counterfactual thought experiment. The juxtaposition of the **Marquis de Sade** (1740-1814), the philosopher of absolute, transgressive freedom through sensual cruelty, and **Søren Kierkegaard** (1813-1855), the philosopher of faith, anxiety, and subjective truth, creates a profound intellectual clash.

If Sade's complete, uncensored writings (like _Philosophy in the Bedroom_, _Justine_, _The 120 Days of Sodom_) had been available to the young Kierkegaard, their impact would have been seismic, likely transforming his work in several dramatic ways:

---

### **1\. The "Aesthetic Stage" Would Have a Radical, Diabolical Extreme**

Kierkegaard's "Aesthetic Stage" (exemplified in _Either/Or_ by figures like the seducer Johannes) is about pleasure, boredom, and ironic detachment. **Sade would provide a fully fleshed-out, philosophical system for the aesthetic pushed to its absolute limit.**

- **Beyond Seduction to Annihilation:** Kierkegaard's aesthete seeks to master through emotion and psychology. Sade's libertine seeks to master through the **destruction of the other's will and body**, treating people as entirely instrumental objects. Kierkegaard might have created a pseudonym (perhaps a "Demoniac" or "Nihilist") to articulate this position, showing the aesthetic not as charmingly melancholic, but as **monstrous and logically consistent in its rejection of all binding norms**.
- **The "System" of Transgression:** Sade presents a cold, Enlightenment-style rationality applied to desire. This would give Kierkegaard a **powerful, systematic antithesis** to faith, more robust than Hegelian abstraction. The struggle would no longer be just between ethics and faith (as in _Fear and Trembling_), but between **two absolute, irreconcilable singularities: the Sadean Sovereign and the Knight of Faith.**

### **2\. A Sharper, More Terrifying Concept of "Despair" and "The Demonic"**

Kierkegaard defined despair as the "sickness unto death" and the demonic as "the unfree disclosure of the closed." Sade's universe is the **literary and philosophical embodiment of these states.**

- **Despair as a Program:** Sade's heroes actively choose and celebrate the despair of **not wanting to be oneself** (the despair of defiance) by annihilating their own capacity for sympathy and constructing a self based on pure, sovereign will. Kierkegaard's analysis in _The Sickness Unto Death_ would have a concrete, extreme case study.
- **The Demonic as Socially Constructed:** Sade’s visions aren't just of isolated monsters, but of **entire social and political orders** (like the castles in _120 Days_) built on systematic transgression. This would allow Kierkegaard to critique not just individual despair, but **demonic systems**—a powerful tool later used by thinkers like the Frankfurt School (Adorno/Horkheimer's _Dialectic of Enlightenment_).

### **3\. A Transformed Dialectic: The Aesthetic-Ethical-Religious Triad**

With Sade in the picture, the "leap" between stages becomes more desperate and absolute.

- **The Ethical as a More Fragile Barrier:** Judge Wilhelm's ethical life (marriage, duty, social order) would appear not just as boring to the aesthete, but as the **primary target for annihilation** by the Sadean libertine. The ethical becomes a **battleground**, not just a choice.
- **Faith in a More Extreme Shadow:** Abraham's "teleological suspension of the ethical" is a silent, agonizing sacrifice for a divine command. The Sadean hero **suspends the ethical for a personal, anti-divine whim of absolute cruelty.** Placing these two figures side-by-side would sharpen Kierkegaard's point exponentially: **faith is not transgression;** it is a qualitatively different category. Faith's "absurd" would be defined in stark opposition to Sade's "horrific."

### **4\. A New Critique of Christendom and "Tamed" Christianity**

Kierkegaard attacked the Danish Lutheran Church for making Christianity comfortable and bourgeois.

- **Sade as the Ultimate _Offense_:** Sade represents the offensive (_skandalon_) core of Christianity pushed to its logical, **inverted extreme**. For Sade, nature's only law is destruction and pleasure; therefore, virtue is weakness and cruelty is supreme. Kierkegaard could use Sade to argue: _"You think Christianity is about being nice and moral? You have not even understood the depth of the offense you are trying to smooth over. Here is the true, logical opposite of Christ's command to love—a philosophy of hate. Until you confront \_this_, your tame faith is meaningless."\_
- **The Figure of Christ vs. The Sadean Tyrant:** The collision would force a deeper articulation of Christ as the **"absolute paradox"**—not just God-man, but the **apotheosis of suffering love** standing against the Sadean apotheosis of inflicting suffering. Christ's cry of dereliction on the cross ("My God, why have you forsaken me?") would be the direct antithesis of the Sadean libertine's triumphant, godless solitude.

### **Probable Outcome and Literary Form**

Kierkegaard would likely **not** have written a direct treatise on Sade. Instead, he would have created a new, terrifying pseudonymous author—a **"Sadean Knight"**—who writes with chilling, logical brilliance. This text would be the ultimate aesthetic work, a _"Diabolical Fragments"_ or _"System of Sovereign Pleasure."_ It would then be answered, indirectly, by the religious writings of Anti-Climacus (_The Sickness Unto Death_, _Practice in Christianity_), with the unspoken presence of the Sadean system as the true enemy of faith.

---

### **Conclusion: A Darker, More Radical Kierkegaard**

The infusion of Sadean thought would have:

- **Intensified** the stakes of his entire authorship.
- **Provided** the most severe possible contrast to the Christian ideal of love and faith.
- **Forced** a more radical, less socially reconcilable concept of religious commitment.
- **Made** his work even more relevant to the 20th century, which grappled directly with the systematic, bureaucratic evil he would have prefigured through this lens.

In essence, **Sade would have been Kierkegaard's ultimate _negative catalyst_. By confronting the pure philosophy of instrumental cruelty and absolute egoism, Kierkegaard's vision of faith as a passionate, subjective relationship with the absolute would have been forged in a far more terrifying and illuminating fire.** The resulting philosophy would be a crucial bridge between 19th-century theology and the 20th-century's confrontation with totalitarianism and nihilism.

# What is Abraham's story? The God of Abraham is "the terrible God."

---

This is a profound pairing of questions that goes to the very heart of Kierkegaard's most famous work, _**Fear and Trembling**_. Let's break it down.

### **Abraham's Story (The "Binding of Isaac" - Genesis 22)**

The biblical narrative is stark and terrifying in its simplicity:

1.  **The Command:** God calls to Abraham, his faithful servant, and says: **"Take your son, your only son Isaac, whom you love, and go to the land of Moriah, and offer him there as a burnt offering."**
2.  **The Obedience:** Without protest or hesitation recorded in the text, Abraham rises early, saddles his donkey, takes Isaac and two servants, splits the wood for the fire, and sets out on a three-day journey.
3.  **The Ascent:** Abraham tells the servants to wait. He lays the wood on Isaac, takes the fire and the knife, and the two walk on together. Isaac asks: "Father, here is the fire and the wood, but where is the lamb for the burnt offering?" Abraham replies, **"God himself will provide the lamb, my son."**
4.  **The Binding:** They arrive. Abraham builds an altar, arranges the wood, **binds Isaac (hence the term _Akedah_ in Hebrew), and lays him on the altar.** He stretches out his hand and takes the knife to slay his son.
5.  **The Intervention:** The angel of the Lord calls out: **"Abraham, Abraham! Do not lay your hand on the boy or do anything to him; for now I know that you fear God, since you have not withheld your son, your only son, from me."**
6.  **The Substitute:** Abraham sees a ram caught in a thicket and offers it as the burnt offering instead of his son.

On the surface, it's a story of ultimate testing and obedience. For philosophers and theologians, it is **the paradigm of religious paradox.**

---

### **"The God of Abraham is 'the terrible God.'" — Kierkegaard's Interpretation**

This phrase captures Kierkegaard's radical reading in _Fear and Trembling_. He is not interested in the happy ending (the ram); he is obsessed with the **three days of silence and the raising of the knife.**

For Kierkegaard, Abraham's God is "terrible" because He demands what is **ethically monstrous and personally catastrophic:**

1.  **The Suspension of the Ethical:** The universal ethical law is: "A father must love and protect his son." Murder, especially of one's child, is the greatest crime. God's command to Abraham is **not a higher ethical duty** (like a soldier sacrificing for his country). It is a **private, teleological suspension of the ethical**—a halt of the universal for a singular, higher relationship to the Absolute (God). Abraham must, from the perspective of ethics, become a **murderer**. This is what makes him a "knight of faith," not a "tragic hero."
2.  **The Incommunicable Paradox:** Abraham's ordeal is **absolutely private**. He cannot explain himself to anyone—not to Sarah, not to Isaac, not to his community. Any explanation ("God told me to") would either be met with disbelief or would turn God into a pagan deity demanding child sacrifice, which He is not. **He must bear the silence.** This isolates him utterly.
3.  **The Absurdity of Faith:** After the command, Abraham’s faith has a double movement:
    - **Resignation:** He must give up Isaac, resign himself to the loss of everything he loves and every promise God had made through Isaac.
    - **Faith by Virtue of the Absurd:** Simultaneously, he must believe **"by virtue of the absurd"** that he will somehow get Isaac back _in this life_. This is not a belief in an afterlife, but an insane, contradictory hope that the very act of obedient sacrifice will not result in loss. As he tells Isaac, "God will provide the lamb." This is the core of the absurd—holding two impossible truths together.

4.  **"The Terrible" vs. "The Tame":** Kierkegaard raged against the "bourgeois" Christianity of his day that turned this terrifying story of anguish and paradox into a simple lesson about obedience with a nice ending. For him, to domesticate this story is to miss everything. **The terror is the point.** It reveals faith not as a warm feeling or moral certainty, but as a **passionate, solitary, and frightful commitment to the Absolute that defies all human reason and social norms.**

### **The Sadean Connection (From Our Previous Thread)**

This is where your two questions collide brilliantly. In our thought experiment, the **Sadean figure** also performs a "suspension of the ethical," but for a diametrically opposite reason and end.

- **Abraham** suspends the ethical (**"Thou shalt not kill"**) **in obedience to a divine command** he cannot understand, driven by a terrifying, transcendent relationship.
- **The Sadean Libertine** suspends the ethical (**"Thou shalt not kill/ torture"**) **in obedience to his own sovereign will and nature's law of cruelty**, driven by a terrifying, immanent desire.

**Both are "terrible."** Both stand as absolute singularities outside universal morality. For Kierkegaard, this contrast would throw the nature of Abraham's faith into the sharpest possible relief: it is **not about transgressive self-assertion, but about a self-annihilating obedience that hopes for a return of everything only by virtue of the absurd.**

**In short: The "terrible God" is the God who demands the impossible, the incomprehensible, and the ethically outrageous, placing the individual in a state of absolute responsibility and absolute isolation—the very furnace in which Kierkegaard believed true faith is forged.**

# Other similar tragedies to Kierkegaard: The family mortality was exceptionally high, even for the era, and was interpreted by Søren as a sign of a divine "curse."

---

Indeed, Kierkegaard's personal narrative of familial loss and perceived divine judgment resonates with a number of other tragic figures in philosophy, literature, and history. The combination of **high familial mortality**, a **domineering, guilt-ridden father**, and the **internalization of this trauma as a metaphysical or spiritual curse** is a powerful and recurring pattern.

Here are several notable figures whose life stories echo Kierkegaard's personal tragedy:

### 1\. **Fyodor Dostoevsky (1821–1881)**

- **The Tragedy:** His mother died of tuberculosis when he was 15. His father, a violent and suspicious alcoholic, was murdered by his own serfs (allegedly in retaliation for his cruelty) when Dostoevsky was 18. He was later sentenced to death and endured a last-minute reprieve to Siberian exile.
- **The "Curse" & Philosophy:** Dostoevsky grappled intensely with guilt, suffering, and the problem of evil. His father's violent death haunted him and is reflected in the parricide at the heart of _The Brothers Karamazov_. Like Kierkegaard, he explored the depths of despair, freedom, and the necessity of faith forged in the crucible of suffering. His "curse" was more social and psychological, but the shadow of a guilty, annihilated patriarch is central to his work.

### 2\. **Franz Kafka (1883–1924)**

- **The Tragedy:** While not marked by high physical mortality, Kafka's family life was a **spiritual and emotional deathscape**. His overbearing, successful father, Hermann, psychologically crushed him, leading Kafka to view himself as a weak, guilty failure. His three younger brothers died in infancy, leaving him the only son with three sisters, a dynamic of survivor's guilt.
- **The "Curse" & Philosophy:** Kafka internalized his familial dynamic as a cosmic curse of judgment and alienation. His entire oeuvre—_The Trial_, _The Castle_, _The Metamorphosis_—is a testament to the individual trapped by incomprehensible, arbitrary authority (a direct reflection of the "terrible" father/God) and burdened by a guilt he cannot name. This mirrors Kierkegaard's sense of being under a divine verdict.

### 3\. **Friedrich Nietzsche (1844–1900)**

- **The Tragedy:** His father, a Lutheran pastor, died of a "softening of the brain" when Nietzsche was four. His younger brother died shortly after. He was raised in a household of five devout women. His family history was plagued by mental illness and early death.
- **The "Curse" & Philosophy:** Nietzsche was obsessed with the idea of **decadence, degeneration, and physiological decline** as a kind of hereditary curse. His own deteriorating health fueled his philosophy of life-affirmation (_amor fati_) as a heroic struggle against a biological and spiritual fate. He directly confronted the "death of God," the void left by the collapse of a moral order—a crisis Kierkegaard foresaw but answered with a leap of faith, whereas Nietzsche called for creating new values beyond good and evil.

### 4\. **Edgar Allan Poe (1809–1849)**

- **The Tragedy:** His father abandoned the family; his mother died of tuberculosis when he was two. He was then separated from his siblings. His foster mother and his young wife, Virginia, also died of tuberculosis. Death, especially of beautiful women, stalked his life.
- **The "Curse" & Philosophy:** Poe's life and work are the very embodiment of the **Gothic curse**. He saw the world as fundamentally macabre and haunted. His stories explore premature burial, madness, and the haunting return of the dead—a direct literary transmutation of personal loss into universal horror. Unlike Kierkegaard's theological framework, Poe's curse was more aesthetic and psychological, but the atmosphere of inescapable doom is parallel.

### 5\. **Blaise Pascal (1623–1662)**

- **The Tragedy:** His mother died when he was three. He was a child prodigy with intensely demanding, controlling father who oversaw his education. He suffered a mysterious paralytic illness at 18 and lived in chronic pain. He experienced a profound, traumatic religious conversion (the "Night of Fire").
- **The "Curse" & Philosophy:** Pascal shared Kierkegaard's sense of human wretchedness, the terror of infinity, and the necessity of a passionate, wager-based faith against a backdrop of existential dread. His _Pensées_ grapple with a hidden God (_Deus absconditus_) and the human condition as one of fallen grandeur. The combination of intellectual genius, physical suffering, and a transformative religious crisis creates a strong kinship.

### 6\. **John Calvin (1509–1564) - A Theological Forerunner**

- **The Tragedy:** While not his personal family, Calvin's entire theological system is built upon the concept of a **divine curse**: the **Total Depravity** of humanity following the Fall of Adam. His own life was marked by exile, persecution, and the death of his only child in infancy.
- **The "Curse" & Philosophy:** Calvin’s God is supremely sovereign and inscrutable, whose election and reprobation are terrifying mysteries. Kierkegaard’s father’s primitive, Jutland-heath conception of a cursing God is a raw, personal version of Calvin's systematic theology. Both present a deity whose demands are absolute and whose judgments inspire fear and trembling.

---

### **Common Threads: The Architecture of a "Curse"**

These figures share a pattern that Kierkegaard epitomizes:

1.  **The Shattered Family:** Early, multiple, or traumatic deaths, especially of parents or siblings.

The modern "place of speech" and its "soft angels" offer **symbolic solutions** (understanding, recognition, reform). Kierkegaard’s world admitted only **real problems** (death, guilt, eternal judgment). His immobility is the only possible fidelity to that reality.

**In the end, Kierkegaard's tragedy is unique because it is the only one that refuses to become a story.** It insists on remaining a **condition**. While others use their place of speech to change the scene, Kierkegaard uses his silence and his writing to **inscribe the scene permanently into existence**, so that none may look away from what he saw: a world where angels do not intervene, where God lets die, and where the only honest response is to stay, anguish, and repeat.

# What Lacan wrote about Kierkegaard?

---

Jacques Lacan engaged with Søren Kierkegaard throughout his career, weaving him into the very fabric of his psychoanalytic project. Kierkegaard wasn't just a reference; he was a **fellow traveler in the territory of desire, anxiety, and the impossibility of direct relation to the Other**.

Here’s what Lacan wrote and said about Kierkegaard, organized by key concepts:

---

### \*\*1. **Anxiety (_Angoisse_) & _The Concept of Anxiety_ (1844)**

This is the most profound point of intersection. Lacan dedicated **Seminar X: _Anxiety_ (1962-63)** to the subject and repeatedly turned to Kierkegaard.

- **Lacan’s Claim:** He called Kierkegaard **"the most acute of the philosophers" on anxiety**, praising him for distinguishing anxiety (_angest_) from fear (_frygt_). Fear has an object (a threat); anxiety is **objectless**—it is the confrontation with the _desire of the Other_ and the threat of the _loss of the loss itself_ (the disappearance of the object-cause of desire, _objet a_).
- **Key Quote (Seminar X):** _"Kierkegaard is the one who posed the question of anxiety in its purest, most radical form... Anxiety is not without an object, but this object is unknown; it is the object of desire insofar as it is not known."_
- **The "Dizziness of Freedom":** Lacan adopted Kierkegaard's famous metaphor of anxiety as the "dizziness of freedom"—the vertigo one feels before the abyss of one's own possibilities. For Lacan, this is the subject before the void of its own desire, faced with the terrifying freedom to choose its own fundamental fantasy.

---

### \*\*2. **Repetition & the Ethics of the Act**

Lacan's concept of **repetition compulsion** (_Wiederholungszwang_) was deeply inflected by Kierkegaard's 1843 book _Repetition_.

- **Repetition vs. Recollection:** Kierkegaard contrasts the Greek philosophical ideal (Platonic recollection of eternal truths) with the **Christian-existential "repetition"**—a movement forward, a passionate striving to regain something in a new way, which constitutes the self. Lacan saw this as a key to the psychoanalytic process: not to remember the past, but to **reconfigure one's relation to it in the present**—to "traverse the fantasy."
- **The "Act" (_l'acte_):** Lacan’s notion of the true Act (which changes the subject's symbolic position) echoes Kierkegaard's "leap." Both are **non-rational, non-guaranteed movements** that break a deadlock. For Kierkegaard, it's the leap of faith; for Lacan, it's the moment when the analysand acts in a way that is not determined by their symptom.

---

### \*\*3. **Desire, Love, and the "Woman"/"Poet"**

In **Seminar XX: _Encore_ (1972-73)**, Lacan links Kierkegaard to the impossible relation between the sexes and the nature of love.

- **The "Poet" and the "Young Girl":** Lacan analyzes Kierkegaard's _The Seducer's Diary_ (from _Either/Or_) not as a manual of seduction, but as a study of **desire as essentially the desire of the Other's desire**. The aesthete Johannes doesn't want the girl (Cordelia); he wants to **be the cause of her desire**, to orchestrate her becoming. This is a pure Lacanian clinic of desire as metonymy.
- **Love as Giving What One Does Not Have:** Lacan saw in Kierkegaard's religious writings (_Works of Love_) a model of love that aligns with his own formula: "to love is to give what one does not have." Divine love, for Kierkegaard, is a **gift that comes from lack**; it is not a possession but a relation. This mirrors the analytic love (_agape_) that is not about the analyst's substance but about their positioned desire.

---

### \*\*4. **The "Sin of the Father" and the Symbolic Debt**

Lacan saw Kierkegaard's personal history—the **cursing father**, the dead siblings—as a live case study in the transmission of the **symbolic debt**.

- **Inherited Sin as Symbolic Structure:** Kierkegaard's concept of "inherited sin" (_arvesynd_) wasn't biological for Lacan; it was about how **the subject is born into a pre-existing field of desire and guilt**—the desire of the parents, their _jouissance_, their unconscious debts. Michael Pedersen Kierkegaard's curse was a **primordial signifier** that organized Søren's entire psychic reality. Lacan would call this the **paternal _sinthome_**—the unique, stabilizing knot of his father's madness that Søren inherited as his existential mission.
- **Psychosis and the "Foreclosed Name-of-the-Father":** Lacan hinted that Kierkegaard's father operated in a **borderline psychotic structure** (foreclosing the symbolic Father's law, leading to a direct, terrifying relation to God-as-Real). Søren’s entire authorship can be read as his attempt to **build a symbolic system (his philosophy) to plug the hole left by this foreclosure**.

---

### \*\*5. **Faith, the "Real," and the "Knight of Faith"**

Lacan treated Kierkegaard's religious stage as a confrontation with the **Real**—that which is outside symbolization.

- **Abraham as the Model of Subjective Destitution:** In _Fear and Trembling_, Abraham’s readiness to sacrifice Isaac represents, for Lacan, the **absolute suspension of all symbolic supports** (ethics, family, reason). Abraham stands alone before the terrifying, incomprehensible desire of God (the big Other). This is the Lacanian subject at the moment of **subjective destitution**, reduced to pure desire of the Other.
- **Faith as a Relation to the Impossible:** Lacan saw Kierkegaard's faith not as belief in dogma, but as a **passionate holding-open to the impossible**, a relationship to an Other whose desire is fundamentally enigmatic. This is precisely the structure of the analytic experience: maintaining a desire for the truth of one's desire, without ever fully knowing the Other's desire.

---

### **Summary: Lacan's Kierkegaard**

For Lacan, Kierkegaard was:

- **The Philosopher of Anxiety**, who understood its objectless, existential nature.
- **The Theorist of Repetition**, who saw the future-oriented reconfiguration of the past as key to subjectivity.
- **The Clinic of Desire**, whose "seduction" is a master study in the metonymy of wanting to be wanted.
- **The Diagnostician of Paternal Transmission**, whose personal history illustrated the inheritance of a symbolic-symptomatic debt.
- **The Explorer of the Act**, whose "leap of faith" is a paradigm for the psychoanalytic passage à l'acte that changes everything.

**In essence, Lacan treated Kierkegaard as a pre-psychoanalytic psychoanalyst of the highest order—one who mapped the terrain of the inner conflict, the encounter with the Other, and the paradoxes of desire with a clinical precision that Freud himself would later systematize, but never surpass in existential intensity.** Lacan’s writing on Kierkegaard is less a critique and more a **recognition of a foundational precursor in the science of the subject**.

# Kierkegaard's main heirs and what they wrote about them?

---

Kierkegaard’s philosophical legacy is immense but indirect; he is a **hidden father** whose DNA permeates 20th and 21st-century thought, especially existentialism, phenomenology, theology, and postmodernism. His heirs did not merely write _about_ him—they **assimilated, transformed, and radicalized his central problems**.

Here are the main heirs and their core engagements:

---

### \*\*1. **Existentialism: The Explicit Heirs**

These thinkers took Kierkegaard’s focus on the individual, anxiety, and choice as the foundation for a full philosophical movement.

- **Martin Heidegger (1889–1976)**

- **Key Work:** _Being and Time_ (1927)
- **Heir to:** Kierkegaard’s concepts of **anxiety (_Angst_)**, **authenticity**, **the crowd (_das Man_)** vs. the individual, and **temporality**.
- **What he wrote/said:** Heidegger explicitly credits Kierkegaard in _Being and Time_ for his "penetrating" analysis of anxiety and the concept of "the moment" (_Augenblick_). However, he criticizes Kierkegaard for remaining within a **"subjectivistic" and theological framework**, whereas Heidegger seeks an **ontological** analysis of _Dasein_ (human existence). For Heidegger, Kierkegaard is the deepest 19th-century psychologist of existence, but not a fundamental ontologist.

- **Jean-Paul Sartre (1905–1980)**

- **Key Work:** _Being and Nothingness_ (1943)
- **Heir to:** Kierkegaard’s **radical freedom**, **despair**, **the necessity of choice**, and the critique of systems.
- **What he wrote/said:** Sartre secularizes Kierkegaard. What for Kierkegaard is "faith" becomes for Sartre the **project**—the free, groundless act by which the self defines itself. Kierkegaard’s "anguish before God" becomes Sartre’s **"anguish of freedom."** Sartre’s famous line _"existence precedes essence"_ is a direct secularization of Kierkegaard’s attack on Hegelian essentialism. He rarely writes extensive commentary on Kierkegaard but absorbs him via Heidegger.

- **Albert Camus (1913–1960)**

- **Key Works:** _The Myth of Sisyphus_ (1942), _The Rebel_ (1951)
- **Heir to:** Kierkegaard’s **absurd** (the confrontation between human need for meaning and the silent universe) and the figure of the **"knight of faith"** (which Camus transforms into the **"absurd hero"**).
- **What he wrote/said:** In _The Myth of Sisyphus_, Camus directly critiques Kierkegaard’s "leap of faith" as **"philosophical suicide"**—a betrayal of the absurd by fleeing into the irrational comfort of God. For Camus, one must live _with_ the absurd, without hope or consolation. Yet, Camus’s **absurd hero** is a direct descendent of Kierkegaard’s **"single individual"** who defiantly affirms his existence in the face of meaninglessness.

---

### \*\*2. **Dialectical Theology / Neo-Orthodoxy: The Theological Heirs**

These theologians used Kierkegaard to attack liberal, rationalist Protestantism and recover a God of radical transcendence and paradox.

- **Karl Barth (1886–1968)**

- **Key Work:** _The Epistle to the Romans_ (1919, 1922)
- **Heir to:** Kierkegaard’s **"infinite qualitative distinction"** between time and eternity, God and humanity, and his critique of "Christendom."
- **What he wrote:** Barth’s famous declaration of God as **"Wholly Other"** is pure Kierkegaard. He saw Kierkegaard as a **"corrective"** against the domestication of God into culture and morality. However, Barth later criticized Kierkegaard for an **excessive subjectivism** that risked making faith a human possibility rather than a gift of divine grace. For Barth, Kierkegaard was a prophetic gadfly, not a systematic dogmatician.

- **Rudolf Bultmann (1884–1976)**

- **Key Concept: Demythologization**
- **Heir to:** Kierkegaard’s **existential interpretation** of Scripture—the idea that truth is not doctrinal but **subjective appropriation**.
- **What he wrote:** Bultmann fused Kierkegaard with Heidegger. For Bultmann, the New Testament’s mythical world-picture must be stripped away to reveal its **existential call to decision**, a Kierkegaardian "either/or" before God. Faith is not belief in facts but a radical transformation of self-understanding.

---

### \*\*3. **Psychoanalysis & Critical Theory: The Unconscious Heirs**

These thinkers absorbed Kierkegaard’s insights into the depths of the psyche and the pathologies of modern society.

- **Ludwig Binswanger (1881–1966) – _Daseinsanalysis_**

- **Heir to:** Kierkegaard’s descriptions of **despair, anxiety, and the stages of existence**.
- **What he wrote:** Binswanger directly applied Kierkegaard’s existential categories to psychopathology. He saw neurosis and psychosis as **distortions of the fundamental "world-designs"** (aesthetic, ethical, religious) that Kierkegaard outlined. For Binswanger, therapy involved helping patients move from inauthentic (aesthetic) modes of being toward more authentic (ethical/religious) ones.

- **Theodor W. Adorno (1903–1969)**

- **Key Work:** _Kierkegaard: Construction of the Aesthetic_ (1933)
- **Heir to:** Kierkegaard’s **critique of mass society** and **dialectical thinking**.
- **What he wrote:** Adorno’s early, dense book is a major Marxist critique. He praises Kierkegaard’s attack on Hegel’s system and his emphasis on **non-identity** (the particular that resists the universal). But Adorno attacks Kierkegaard’s **"inwardness"** as a bourgeois illusion—a retreat into the private that abandons the material, social world to domination. For Adorno, Kierkegaard’s subject is a **"frozen protest"** that ultimately colludes with the very alienation it decries.

---

### \*\*4. **Postmodernism & Deconstruction: The Ironic Heirs**

These thinkers saw in Kierkegaard a master of indirect communication, paradox, and the subversion of philosophical certainty.

- **Jacques Derrida (1930–2004)**

- **Key Text:** _The Gift of Death_ (1992)
- **Heir to:** Kierkegaard’s **secrecy, responsibility, and the sacrifice of Isaac**.
- **What he wrote:** In _The Gift of Death_, Derrida performs a deconstructive reading of _Fear and Trembling_. He focuses on the **absolute secrecy** of Abraham’s decision and the **paradox of responsibility**: that to be responsible to God (the absolute Other), one must be irresponsible to the ethical (the universal). For Derrida, this reveals that **all responsibility involves a secret, non-justifiable sacrifice of other others**. Kierkegaard becomes the thinker of the **aporia of ethics**.

- **Slavoj Žižek (b. 1949)**

- **Key References:** Throughout his work, especially _The Parallax View_ (2006)
- **Heir to:** Kierkegaard’s **"leap," the "teleological suspension of the ethical," and the critique of Hegel**.
- **What he writes:** Žižek, reading Kierkegaard through Lacan and Hegel, sees him as the thinker of **the Act**—a radical, traumatic intervention that changes the very coordinates of reality. He reads Kierkegaard’s "leap of faith" not as a religious move, but as a **political one**: the moment when one acts _as if_ freedom is possible, thereby creating it. For Žižek, Kierkegaard is the antidote to Hegelian " reconciliation," insisting on the **irreducible rupture** of subjectivity.

---

### \*\*5. **Contemporary Philosophy & Theology: The Living Heirs**

- **John D. Caputo (b. 1940) – "Weak Theology"**

- **Key Work:** _How to Read Kierkegaard_ (2007)
- **Heir to:** Kierkegaard’s **passion, paradox, and the "leap."**
- **What he writes:** Caputo reads Kierkegaard as a poet of existential risk, a thinker of **"the perhaps"**—the maybe of faith in a postmodern, uncertain world. He downplays doctrine and emphasizes the **passionate pursuit of the impossible**.

- **Clare Carlisle (b. 1977) – Philosopher of Becoming**

- **Key Work:** _Kierkegaard’s Philosophy of Becoming_ (2005)
- **Heir to:** Kierkegaard’s **dynamic, process-oriented view of the self**.
- **What she writes:** Carlisle emphasizes Kierkegaard as a philosopher of **movement, repetition, and transformation**, aligning him with thinkers like Bergson and Deleuze, and rescuing him from the static "stages" interpretation.

- **1809–1837:** After his mother’s death, the family moved a short distance to **Nytorv 21** (corner of Nytorv and Østergade). His father died here in 1838.

- **1837–1840:** After his father’s death (1838), Søren inherited a substantial fortune. He left the family home and moved into his own apartment at **Løngangstræde 9**. - **1844–1845:** Moved to **Rosenborggade 156**. A period of staggering productivity. Here, he wrote _Philosophical Fragments_, _The Concept of Anxiety_, and _Stages on Life’s Way_. The apartment was a **literary fortress**.
- **1845–1847:** Returned to **Nytorv 2**, his childhood home (now owned by his brother Peter Christian). He lived in a second-floor apartment overlooking the square. Wrote _Concluding Unscientific Postscript_ here. Living in the shadow of his past, he completed his pseudonymous authorship.

- **1852–1855:** His final and most famous address: **Østergade 108–110 (on the corner of Kjøbmagergade)**. He lived in a first-floor apartment here, in the very heart of Copenhagen. This was the **command center for his final, all-out attack on the Danish State Church**. From these windows, he could see the city he was scandalizing. Here, he wrote the furious pamphlets of **"The Moment"** (_Øjeblikket_). The apartment was sparsely furnished, dominated by his writing desk.

He refused to leave for his home, seeing the hospital as a fittingly stark and public place for his final act. He died here in **Room 1** (or possibly Room 6; sources vary), attended by his friend Emil Boesen. His last view was not of his study, but of a hospital wall.

# Søren Kierkegaard's sexuality

---

The question of **Søren Kierkegaard's sexuality** is a complex and contested area of scholarship, intersecting his biography, psychology, and philosophy. There is no evidence of any sexual or romantic relationship in his life beyond his famously broken engagement to **Regine Olsen**. The debate centers on interpreting his **profound ambivalence toward marriage, the erotic, and the body**—an ambivalence he theorized extensively.

Here is a breakdown of the key evidence and interpretations:

---

### **1\. The Known Fact: The Engagement to Regine Olsen (1840–1841)**

- **The Event:** At 27, he proposed to 18-year-old Regine. He then deliberately broke the engagement, orchestrating a scandal to make himself appear a scoundrel and "free" her.
- **His Stated Reasons (in his journals):** A combination of:
  - **Melancholy & Guilt:** His inherited "melancholy" and his father's "curse" made him unfit for marital happiness.
  - **Religious Vocation:** He felt a calling to a singular, suffering authorship that required solitude. Marriage was part of the "universal" (ethical stage) he felt compelled to suspend.
  - **Protection of Regine:** He believed dragging her into his tortured inner world would destroy her.

- **Aftermath:** He loved her until his death (he willed his estate to her), made her the **silent dedicatee** of his entire authorship, and saw the relationship as the central existential crisis of his life. This intense, yet unconsummated and sacrificed, love is the _only_ documented romantic/erotic attachment.

---

### **2\. The Philosophical & Psychological Evidence**

#### **A. Fear of the Erotic & the "Seducer's Diary"**

- In _Either/Or_, the aesthetic life is epitomized by "Johannes the Seducer." The seduction is **psychological, not physical**; the goal is to poetically "create" a love affair and abandon it at the peak to preserve it as an idea. This is often read as Kierkegaard's **exorcism of his own aesthetic potential**—a mapping of a path he rejected.
- He consistently associates immediate sensual pleasure with **despair, boredom, and inauthenticity**. The bodily and erotic are traps that prevent the "leap" to higher (ethical, religious) existence.

#### **B. The Body as Problematic**

- Kierkegaard had a **malformed, slightly hunched back** (likely from Pott's disease or scoliosis) and was physically frail. He was acutely self-conscious about his body, referring to himself as a "pen" in the hand of God, suggesting a disembodied instrumentality.
- His philosophy emphasizes **inwardness, spirit, and subjectivity** over the physical. The self in _The Sickness Unto Death_ is a relation of the **psychical and the physical** that relates itself to itself—but the synthesis is fraught, and despair often manifests as a rejection of the bodily.

#### **C. Ambivalence Toward Marriage & Reproduction**

- He viewed marriage as the cornerstone of the **ethical stage** (Judge Wilhelm in _Either/Or_ praises it). Yet, he portrayed it as a potentially dull, spirit-numbing form of social conformity.
- His refusal to marry and have children can be seen as:
  1.  A **spiritual sacrifice** (like a monastic celibacy for his vocation).
  2.  A **psychic impossibility** due to melancholy and guilt.
  3.  A **philosophical stance** against the "universal" life path.
  4.  A **literal enactment** of his family's "curse," letting the lineage die out.

---

### **3\. Scholarly Speculations & Debates**

#### **A. The Ascetic / Religious Celibate Hypothesis (Majority View)**

- Kierkegaard consciously **sublimated** all erotic energy into his writing and religious passion. His "love affair" was with God and his readers. This aligns with a long Christian ascetic tradition where sexuality is renounced for a higher spiritual calling. His sexuality was **not repressed but redirected**.

#### **B. The Latent Homosexuality Hypothesis (Controversial)**

- Proposed by some scholars (e.g., **Joakim Garff** in his biography _SAK_). Evidence cited includes:
  - Intense, admiring friendships with younger men (e.g., **Emil Boesen**, his confidant; the student **Frederik Sibbern**).
  - Aesthetic fascination with male figures (Don Juan, the seducer).
  - A "panic" fear of female sexuality and reproduction, seen as a threat to his intellectual/spiritual purity.
  - His philosophical focus on **"the exception," "the single one,"** and a rejection of the heterosexual, reproductive norm.

- **Critique:** This is largely speculative and **anachronistic**, applying modern sexual categories to a 19th-century figure for whom such an identity did not exist. His male friendships fit the period's model of deep, non-sexual romantic friendship (_"Brodermensch"_). Most scholars see this as a minority, suggestive, but ultimately unprovable reading.

#### **C. The Asexual / Anhedonic Hypothesis**

- That he had **little to no innate sexual drive**. His melancholy, physical ailments, and intellectual intensity may have resulted in a naturally low libido. His obsession with Regine was then primarily **psychological and symbolic**—a love of the _idea_ of love and sacrifice, not a physically expressed desire.

---

### **4\. Lacanian & Psychoanalytic Readings**

- Lacanians see Kierkegaard's sexuality as structured around the **"sinthome"**—his unique psychic knot. The **foreclosed Name-of-the-Father** (his father's curse) created a direct, terrifying relation to God (the big Other), leaving no symbolic space for a "normal" heterosexual union.
- His desire was **cathected onto writing itself**. The pen, the pseudonym, the book became the objects of his libidinal investment. He achieved _jouissance_ not through sex, but through the **agonized production of text** and the staging of his own suffering.

### **Conclusion: The Sexuality of the Exception**

Kierkegaard’s sexuality is ultimately **inscrutable and perhaps irrelevant in modern terms**. What is clear is that he **systematically and philosophically refused normative sexuality** (marriage, family) as part of his lifelong project of becoming the **"exception," the "single individual"** (_den Enkelte_).

- **He eroticized thought and faith.**
- **He made anxiety, not pleasure, the central affect of human existence.**
- **He transformed his own possible "deficiency" into the very engine of his universal philosophy.**

In the end, to ask about Kierkegaard's sexuality is to ask about the **erotics of anguish**. His passion was for the impossible: for God, for an idealized Regine who remained an idea, and for a communication that could only be indirect. His was a sexuality—if we can call it that—of **permanent deferral and sublime sacrifice**, perfectly consistent with his vision of a self that is not a being, but a **relation that relates itself to itself** in the terrifying freedom before God.

# History/genealogy of the terrible God

---

**The genealogy of the "terrible God" is a history of the human encounter with divine violence, arbitrary will, and unbearable silence—an encounter that precedes and shadows the God of love, order, and covenant.**

Let’s trace this lineage, moving from ancient myth to Kierkegaard’s personalization of the archetype:

---

### \*\*I. **Pre-Biblical & Mythic Origins: The Capricious Divine**

- **Mesopotamian & Canaanite Storm Gods:** **Enlil**, **Marduk**, **Baal-Hadad**. Gods of chaos, flood, and thunder who must be appeased. Their power is raw, often indifferent to human morality.
- **The Greek _Deimos_ and _Phobos_ (Terror and Fear):** The sons of Ares, attendants of war. The gods themselves could be sources of arbitrary terror (Zeus’s lightning, Hera’s vengeance).
- **Fate (_Moira_, _Ananke_):** An even more terrifying concept than the gods—an impersonal, inescapable force that even Zeus must obey. This is the **divine as blind, mechanistic doom**.

---

### \*\*II. **The Hebrew Bible’s Duality: The God Who Wounds and Heals**

This is the foundational text for Kierkegaard’s "terrible God." The tension is never resolved.

1.  **The _Elohim_ of Power & Mystery:**
    - **Genesis 22:** The _Akedah_ (Binding of Isaac). God as **the demander of the impossible, the tester** who commands filicide.
    - **Exodus:** The God who hardens Pharaoh’s heart, then drowns his army. The God whose presence on Sinai is so dangerous that boundaries must be set.
    - **The Book of Job:** The core text. God appears in the whirlwind not to justify justice, but to **overwhelm with questions of cosmic power:** _"Where were you when I laid the earth’s foundation?"_ This is divinity as **sublime, terrifying authorship beyond human ethics.**

2.  **The Prophets & The God of Wrath:**
    - **Hosea, Amos, Jeremiah:** God as the betrayed spouse, the furious judge who will destroy his own people, using empires like Assyria and Babylon as his "rod of anger." This is **ethical terror**—punishment for sin—but its severity feels excessive, passionate, terrifying.

3.  **The _Deus Absconditus_ (The Hidden God):**
    - **Isaiah 45:15:** _"Truly, you are a God who hides himself."_ The terror here is **epistemological and existential**—the inability to locate God in disaster, the silence that amplifies anguish.

---

### \*\*III. **Christian Wrestlings: The Crucified God & The Inscrutable Will**

- **Augustine of Hippo:** His doctrine of **Predestination** (later hardened by Calvin) introduces a theological terror: God’s eternal, unchanging decree of election and reprobation. The "terrible God" is the **sovereign who chooses, for inscrutable reasons, to save some and not others**.
- **Martin Luther:** His **"hidden God" (_Deus absconditus_)** is not just concealed but **actively working contradiction**—saving through the cross (foolishness to Greeks), condemning through the law. To stand before this God without the mediation of Christ is to stand before **naked, consuming fire**.
- **Blaise Pascal:** The **"God of Abraham, God of Isaac, God of Jacob, not of the philosophers and scholars."** This is the God of lived, terrifying relationship, of **wagers and existential dread**, not of rational consolation. His _Memorial_ records a night of "FIRE" and terror.

---

### \*\*IV. **Modernity’s Turn: The Internalization of Terror**

- **Baruch Spinoza:** Radically redefines God as **Substance (_Deus sive Natura_)**. The terror becomes **impersonal**: we are modes of an infinite, indifferent system. Freedom is understanding necessity, but the emotional terror of arbitrary will is replaced by the **sublime dread of cosmic insignificance**.
- **Friedrich Nietzsche:** Announces the **"death of God"**—but the terror doesn’t vanish; it **reverts to humanity**. The "terrible" is now the **abyss of meaninglessness**, the "monster of energy" of the eternal return, the weight of creating our own values without a foundation. The divine terror is **immanentized**.

---

### \*\*V. **The 20th Century: The Terrible God After the Camps**

- **Karl Barth:** Reacting to liberal theology, re-emphasizes God as **"Wholly Other"**—the absolute crisis for humanity, the **judge who negates all human projects**. His _Nein!_ to natural theology is a terror of divine transcendence.
- **Elie Wiesel / Holocaust Theology:** The figure of **God hanging on the gallows** in _Night_. The "terrible God" becomes the **God who permits (or is powerless before) radical evil**. The silence at Auschwitz is the modern _Deus absconditus_.
- **Thomas J. J. Altizer / Death of God Theology:** Takes Nietzsche literally: God has died in Christ, emptying Himself into the world. The terror is now the **absence of any transcendent guarantee**, a world of pure immanence and responsibility.

---

### \*\*VI. **The Kierkegaardian Synthesis: The Terrible God as Personal Catalyst**

Kierkegaard **personalizes and existentializes** the entire tradition. His "terrible God" is:

1.  **Inherited:** The God of his father’s Jutland curse—a **familial, psychic reality**.
2.  **The God of the Absolute Paradox:** In _Philosophical Fragments_, God in time is the ultimate offense to reason. This is **epistemological terror**.
3.  **The God of the Single Individual:** Not the God of nations or churches, but the God who addresses **you alone** with an impossible demand (Abraham). This is **terror of singularity**.
4.  **The God of Anguish (_Angest_):** The God before whom one feels the dizziness of freedom and the weight of infinite possibility. This terror is **the very engine of subjectivity**.

For Kierkegaard, the "terrible God" is **not the opposite of the God of love; He is its precondition.** One must pass through the terror of infinite resignation to make the leap to faith. The "knights of faith" are those who maintain a relationship with this terrible God **by virtue of the absurd.**
