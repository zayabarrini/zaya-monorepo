# Nazism, How did intellectuals, artists, business leaders who were against nazism manage to live?

What were the solutions?

---

This is an excellent and complex question that gets to the heart of the difficult choices faced under a totalitarian regime. The experiences of intellectuals, artists, and business leaders who were **opposed to Nazism** varied dramatically based on their personal circumstances, fame, wealth, and the severity of the threat they faced. There was no single solution, but rather a spectrum of strategies for survival and moral navigation.

Here’s a breakdown of their primary options and how they played out:

### 1. **Inner Emigration (Innere Emigration)**

This was the most common path for those who stayed in Germany but withdrew from public life and refused to collaborate.

- **What it was:** A conscious retreat into private life, "inner exile," or an apolitical stance. They would focus on non-political work, often using coded language (Aesopian language) in their art or writing to express dissent subtly.
- **Examples:**
  - **Writer Ernst Jünger:** Wrote allegorical novels like _"On the Marble Cliffs"_ (1939), which could be read as a critique of totalitarianism.
  - **Artist Karl Hofer:** Continued painting in a "degenerate" style but exhibited little, surviving on private sales and teaching.
  - **Composer Carl Orff:** Focused on "apolitical" projects like _"Carmina Burana"_ and managed to avoid writing music for the party, though he navigated a grey area.
- **Challenges:** It was a morally ambiguous position. It required constant compromise and risk. They were often viewed with suspicion by the regime and later criticized by exiles for not taking a stronger stand.

### 2. **Active Resistance**

A small, extremely dangerous minority chose this path.

- **What it was:** Engaging in clandestine activities like distributing leaflets, hiding persecuted people, plotting against Hitler, or sharing intelligence with the Allies.
- **Examples:**
  - **Business Leaders:** Some individuals within **Siemens** or **Bosch** (like Robert Bosch himself) quietly protected Jewish employees or supported resistance figures.
  - **Intellectuals in the Kreisau Circle:** Figures like **Helmuth James Graf von Moltke** and **Peter Yorck von Wartenburg** (aristocrats and intellectuals) planned for a post-Nazi Germany and were executed after the failed July 20, 1944 plot.
  - **The White Rose:** Student intellectuals **Sophie and Hans Scholl**, and their professor **Kurt Huber**, were executed for distributing anti-Nazi leaflets.
- **Consequences:** The risk was execution. Most resistance networks were eventually infiltrated by the Gestapo.

### 3. **Emigration/Exile**

This was the definitive solution for those who could see the writing on the wall and had the means to leave.

- **Who could do it:** It required **foresight, international connections, financial resources, and a host country willing to accept them**. Getting visas was notoriously difficult.
- **Examples:**
  - **Intellectuals & Scientists:** **Albert Einstein**, **Thomas Mann**, **Hannah Arendt**, **Bertolt Brecht**. Many enriched the intellectual life of the US (e.g., the Frankfurt School at Columbia University) and the UK.
  - **Artists:** **Piet Mondrian**, **Paul Klee**, **Marlene Dietrich** (who actively entertained Allied troops).
  - **Business Leaders:** A few, like **Paul Lazarus** (department store executive), fled, often after being forced to sell their assets at a fraction of their value ("Aryanization").
- **Challenges:** Exile often meant poverty, obscurity, language barriers, and profound homesickness. Many struggled to find work and were viewed with suspicion as "enemy aliens" at first.

### 4. **"Swimming Along" (Mitschwimmen) and Calculated Cooperation**

This was a grey zone of minimal compliance for survival, often indistinguishable from passive acceptance to outsiders.

- **What it was:** Doing just enough to avoid trouble—paying party dues, attending mandatory rallies, producing work that was not explicitly anti-Nazi but also not overt propaganda. The goal was to keep one's job, studio, or business open.
- **Examples:**
  - **Conductor Wilhelm Furtwängler:** Remained in Germany, arguing he was preserving German culture. He helped some Jewish musicians but also conducted for Nazi events, creating a lasting controversy about his legacy.
  - **Industrialists in the "Armaments Miracle":** Business leaders like **Albert Vögler** of United Steel Works or those at **IG Farben** prioritized company survival and profit, arguing they were just "following orders" and keeping the economy running, while becoming deeply enmeshed in the war economy and forced labor.
- **Dilemma:** This path often led to **moral corrosion and complicity**. The line between survival and collaboration was razor-thin and easily crossed.

### 5. **Suppression and "Silence as a Weapon"**

For some, the only form of protest available was to stop creating altogether.

- **What it was:** Choosing not to publish, exhibit, or compose rather than create under Nazi dictates. This was a form of professional suicide.
- **Example:** The composer **Anton Webern** lived in poverty and obscurity, writing very little, supported by his family and publisher. He was a man of inner conviction who could not work within the system.

### Key Factors Influencing Their Choice:

- **Race and Heritage:** Jewish individuals, or those married to Jews, had far fewer options and faced existential threat, making emigration or hiding the only paths.
- **International Reputation:** Famous figures like Einstein or Thomas Mann had an easier time securing visas and offers from abroad.
- **Wealth:** Money could buy escape routes, bribe officials, or fund a life of quiet inner emigration.
- **Family Ties:** Responsibility for children, elderly parents, or a non-famous spouse made leaving or resisting much harder.
- **Patriotism:** Some deeply loved Germany and believed they could act as a "better" force from within, or could not imagine life outside their homeland.

### Conclusion:

There was no safe or honorable solution. The choices formed a **spectrum between martyrdom and complicity**:

- **Exile** meant safety but often irrelevance and loss.
- **Inner Emigration** preserved a physical home but required moral compromise and constant anxiety.
- **Resistance** offered moral clarity but almost certain death.
- **Cooperation** ensured survival but at the cost of one's soul and legacy.

Most individuals who stayed navigated a shifting combination of inner emigration and calculated cooperation, making compromises day by day to survive in a system designed to eliminate all opposition and co-opt all talent. Their experiences remain a powerful lesson in the difficult moral calculus of life under totalitarianism.

# What about Psychoanalysts?

---

Excellent and specific question. The situation for psychoanalysts under Nazism was particularly complex and fraught, as their field was politically and ideologically suspect from the start. Their strategies for survival followed the general patterns but with unique professional twists.

### Why Psychoanalysis was a Target:

The Nazi regime viewed psychoanalysis with deep suspicion for several reasons:

1.  **"Jewish Science":** Its founder, Sigmund Freud, was Jewish, and a disproportionately high number of its early practitioners were Jewish or had Jewish heritage. This made it ideologically anathema.
2.  **Focus on Sexuality & the Unconscious:** Its theories conflicted with Nazi ideals of racial purity, Aryan strength, and conscious will. The idea of primal drives and childhood sexuality was seen as degenerate.
3.  **Emphasis on Individuality:** Psychoanalysis focused on the unique inner life of the individual, which was diametrically opposed to the Nazi demand for total subordination of the individual to the _Volk_ (people/nation).

### Strategies for Survival:

#### 1. **Emigration/Exile (The Freudian Path)**

This was the path for the core of the movement, led by Freud himself.

- **Freud's Reluctant Exodus:** Initially resistant to leave Vienna ("The Gestapo will not treat me so ill"), he was finally persuaded after the annexation of Austria (_Anschluss_) in 1938. With the help of his follower (and Nazi sympathizer) **Princess Marie Bonaparte**, and after paying a large "flight tax," he and his immediate family left for London, where he died in 1939.
- **Diaspora:** This created a massive brain drain. Key figures like **Anna Freud**, **Ernst Kris**, **Heinz Hartmann**, **Otto Fenichel**, and **Karen Horney** (who had left earlier) emigrated to the UK, the US, and elsewhere. They re-founded psychoanalysis in the Anglophone world, profoundly influencing post-war psychiatry.

#### 2. **"Gleichschaltung" and Co-optation: The "Göring Institute"**

This is the most fascinating and controversial adaptation. In Germany, psychoanalysis was not simply destroyed; it was forcibly absorbed and transformed.

- **The Key Figure: Matthias Heinrich Göring** (a cousin of Hermann Göring). A psychiatrist with some interest in psychotherapy, he was put in charge of reorganizing all psychotherapy in the Reich.
- **The Strategy:** In 1936, he forced the merger of all German psychotherapy societies into the **"German Institute for Psychological Research and Psychotherapy"** (known as the Göring Institute). Its survival strategy involved:
  - **De-Judaification:** All Jewish members were expelled. The works of Freud were banned from the library.
  - **"Germanization" of Theory:** Freudian terms were replaced. "Psychoanalysis" was downplayed in favor of "**Neopsychoanalysis**" or "**Depth Psychology**" (_Tiefenpsychologie_). The focus shifted from sexuality and the Oedipus complex to concepts of will, leadership, and "community" — bending the science to fit Nazi ideology.
  - **Pragmatic Service:** The Institute made itself useful to the regime. It treated war neuroses ("shell shock") for the Wehrmacht, provided therapy for Nazi elites, and worked on personnel selection. This **pragmatic utility** was its shield.
- **The Moral Ambiguity:** Prominent non-Jewish analysts like **Carl Müller-Braunschweig**, **Felix Boehm**, and **Harald Schultz-Hencke** participated in this to "save" psychotherapy. They argued they were preserving the core of the discipline under the surface. However, this required immense compromise, collaboration, and ethical contortion. After the war, this generation faced bitter recriminations from returning exiles.

#### 3. **Inner Emigration & Private Practice**

Some analysts, particularly in cities like Berlin, attempted to continue a semblance of private practice "in the quiet."

- They saw select, trusted patients.
- They used coded language, avoiding Freudian terminology.
- They lived in constant fear of denunciation by patients or colleagues.
- This was an extremely precarious existence, especially for anyone with a "non-Aryan" spouse or heritage.

#### 4. **Resistance and Catastrophe**

A few analysts were involved in active resistance, with tragic consequences.

- **John Rittmeister:** A Swiss-German psychoanalyst working at the Göring Institute. He and his wife led a resistance group, the **"European Union,"** distributing leaflets and helping persecuted people. He was arrested in 1942 and **executed by guillotine** in 1943.
- **Edith Jacobson:** A Jewish psychoanalyst in Berlin, she was arrested in 1935 for left-wing political activities (helping a patient flee). She escaped from prison in 1938 and eventually made her way to the United States.

### Conclusion: A Profession Under Extreme Duress

The experience of psychoanalysts under Nazism is a stark case study of how a scientific discipline is politicized and must navigate a totalitarian regime. The options were:

- **Pure Exile:** Preserving the doctrine but losing the homeland and institutional base.
- **Corrupted Survival:** Preserving the institution and profession through profound ideological compromise and collaboration, which altered the doctrine itself.
- **Heroic Resistance:** A path chosen by very few, leading almost inevitably to death.

The post-war psychoanalytic community was deeply fractured by these different paths. The "collaborators" of the Göring Institute and the returning exiles viewed each other with immense distrust, leading to decades of conflict within international psychoanalytic associations. The story shows that even the science of the human mind was not immune to the distorting pressures of political terror.

You've articulated a profound and forward-looking insight. The essence of your statement is that **understanding languages, laws, and the cultural "temperature" of major regions is a form of 21st-century preparedness.** It is a strategy for personal and intellectual resilience in a world where political and cultural climates can shift dramatically.

Let's break down your observation and expand on its implications for preparing for the next 50 years.

### The Core Thesis: Linguistic and Cultural Fluency as a Lifeline

Your argument connects historical examples of totalitarianism with a future-oriented strategy:

- **Past:** Under Nazism and other regimes, those with knowledge, networks, and means to cross borders had more options. Language was key to those options.
- **Future:** In a century where geopolitical power is shifting, and ideological pressures may arise in new forms, **multilingual and intercultural literacy becomes a strategic asset.** It allows you to read the room, sense danger early, and have the practical tools to navigate or exit a deteriorating situation.

### Applying Your Framework: The Role of Major Languages & Domains

#### 1. **Languages as Windows and Doors**

- **Hindi (and the broader Indo-Aryan sphere):** As you note, with India's projected economic and demographic rise, Hindi (and its sister languages) is not just a language of a billion people, but a key to understanding the cultural, political, and digital discourse of a future superpower. It offers access to a vast, pluralistic, yet complex democratic space.
- **Chinese (Mandarin):** The language of the other defining 21st-century power. Fluency is less about agreement and more about understanding intent, state narratives, and business realities. It is essential for comprehending a system with a very different concept of law, rights, and individual vs. state.
- **Arabic:** Provides a deep understanding of a civilization at a critical crossroads, spanning from the Atlantic to the Gulf. It's crucial for engaging with energy politics, religious movements, and the rich cultural production of a vast region.
- **European Languages (German, Russian, French, etc.):** These remain languages of critical historical archives, scientific research, legal traditions, and regional politics. Knowing them allows you to learn from Europe's deep, often tragic, experience with totalitarianism and recovery.

#### 2. **International Law as the Rulebook (and Its Limits)**

Knowing international law is knowing the _formal_ rules of the game between states and for human rights.

- **As a Shield:** It provides a framework to articulate grievances, seek asylum, and hold regimes accountable (though enforcement is weak).
- **As a Diagnostic Tool:** Watching which laws a state ignores, twists, or withdraws from is a key indicator of its direction. The erosion of the **Rules-Based International Order** is a major "cultural temperature" shift.
- **As a Practical Tool:** Knowledge of immigration law, extradition treaties, and citizenship rules is literal survival knowledge for a potential "plan B."

#### 3. **Cultural Temperature as the Vital Skill**

This is the most subtle and crucial part. It's **cultural intelligence (CQ)**.

- It’s the ability to sense when the public discourse is shifting from nationalism to xenophobia.
- It’s recognizing the markers of **creeping authoritarianism**: the change in tone of media, the jokes that are no longer made, the academics who suddenly leave, the new laws that are "just for security."
- It’s understanding the **local markers of stability or unrest**—not just from news headlines, but from social media trends, art, cinema, and everyday conversations.

### A Practical Blueprint for the Next 50 Years

Based on your insight, here is how one might prepare:

1.  **Invest in Language Acquisition Strategically:**
    - **Layer 1 (Global Lingua Franca):** English. For now, it remains the operating system of global business, tech, and academia.
    - **Layer 2 (Civilizational Literacy):** Choose **one** from each major sphere of influence: **Mandarin** (Sinosphere), **Hindi/Urdu** (Indosphere), **Arabic** (Islamicate world), and a **European language** of your interest (e.g., German for philosophy/engineering, French for law/diplomacy, Russian for geopolitics).
    - **Goal:** Operational fluency in at least 2-3, with passive/cultural understanding of others.

2.  **Study Systems, Not Just Languages:**
    - Don't just learn to order food. Learn to read a legal document, a news editorial, a social media storm in that language. Understand the local platforms (WeChat, Douyin, Telegram channels, local news sites).

3.  **Map Your "Possible Places to Live":**
    - Identify countries/regions whose values (as codified in their constitution and daily practice) align with yours. Research their **long-term visa pathways** (digital nomad, investor, skilled professional, retirement). **Diversify your geographic footprint** like you would a financial portfolio.

4.  **Build a Transnational Network:**
    - Cultivate genuine friendships and professional connections in different regions. In a crisis, a local contact is worth more than a thousand Google searches. This network is your early-warning system for shifts in "cultural temperature."

5.  **Develop a Portable Skill Set:**
    - Professions that can be performed remotely (tech, design, writing, consulting, certain financial and legal services) provide the ultimate flexibility. Pair this with your linguistic skills to serve multiple markets.

### Conclusion: From Fear to Agency

You begin with "fear of a foreign language," but end with a prescription for empowerment. The goal is to transform the unknown and potentially threatening ("fear of a foreign language") into a toolkit of understanding and access.

**In the 21st century, sovereignty is not just about a passport; it is about optionality.** The person who can understand the political discourse in Delhi, the legal shifts in Brussels, the social mood in São Paulo, and the business climate in Singapore has more options, more clarity, and more freedom than one who is monolingual and monocultural.

Your insight is correct: **In a world of potential closures, the mind that can open the most doors—through language, law, and cultural empathy—will be the most free.** This is not about paranoia, but about prudence and profound engagement with the complex, multipolar world we are entering.

# What are the parallels between Schreber's experience and the Nazism (Totalitarian regime, invasion, delivering a new race, God's will)

This is a fascinating and sophisticated question that connects **psychopathology, political ideology, and cultural history**. The parallels between Daniel Paul Schreber’s experiences (as described in his 1903 _Memoirs of My Nervous Illness_) and the logic of Nazism are striking—not because Schreber was a Nazi (he wasn’t; he died in 1911), but because his psychotic system eerily prefigures core aspects of totalitarian thinking, particularly as embodied by Hitler.

Here’s a breakdown of the parallels you mentioned, plus some deeper thematic ones.

---

### **1. The "Totalitarian" Structure: A Closed, All-Encompassing System**

- **Schreber’s Worldview:** His delusion formed a complete, systematic cosmology. Nothing was random; every sensation, thought, and event was part of a divine plan centered on him. He was perpetually observed, his mind invaded, his body transformed—all according to the laws of "nerve-language" and divine rays.
- **Nazi Totalitarianism:** The regime sought to control all aspects of life (_Gleichschaltung_—coordination). There was no private sphere; everything was political. The ideology explained all of history, current events, and the future through the lens of racial struggle. Individuality was dissolved into the _Volk_.

**Parallel:** Both are **hermetically sealed systems of thought** that admit no external critique. Contradictory facts are either incorporated or destroyed.

---

### **2. Invasion and Violation of the Self/Body Politic**

- **Schreber:** He experienced “soul murder” (_Seelenmord_) and constant invasion by divine “rays” that sought to manipulate his thoughts, transform his body (into a woman), and erase his individuality. Boundaries between self and outside were obliterated.
- **Nazism:** The state invaded private life, demanded total loyalty, and sought to purify the “body politic” of the nation. “Polluting” elements (Jews, Roma, disabled, etc.) were to be removed. The invasion of other nations (Lebensraum) was justified as a biological necessity for the German Volk’s survival.

**Parallel:** The **obsession with purity and violation**. Schreber’s fear of psychic pollution mirrors the Nazi fear of racial pollution. Both involve an existential threat requiring radical, violent cleansing.

---

### **3. Creation of a "New Race" / Transfigured Humanity**

- **Schreber:** Central to his delusion was his transformation into a woman to give birth to a new race of humans, redeemed through him. He was to be the savior-martyr through this “fertilization” by divine rays.
- **Nazism:** Aimed to create a “New Man” (_der neue Mensch_)—a racially pure, physically perfect Aryan, freed from decadent morality. This was to be achieved through eugenics (positive breeding, sterilization of “inferiors”) and extermination of “life unworthy of life.”

**Parallel:** The **messianic project of biological-spiritual renewal**. Both are grandiose, world-historical missions centered on the transformation of humanity’s very substance.

---

### **4. "God's Will" / Divine Mission and Cosmic Law**

- **Schreber:** Believed he was in direct communication with God, and his transformation was according to “the Order of the World.” His suffering had cosmic significance—he was restoring a divine order that had been disturbed.
- **Nazism:** Hitler often used quasi-religious, providential language. The Nazi mission was framed as a **law of nature** (social Darwinism elevated to cosmic principle). Hitler saw himself as the instrument of Providence or Fate, carrying out a historical necessity for the salvation of the Aryan race.

**Parallel:** **Secularized religiosity.** Both replace traditional religion with a pseudo-scientific, cosmic law (Schreber’s nerve-language; Nazi racial science) that justifies extreme action. The leader/figure is the chosen interpreter of this higher law.

---

### **5. Paranoia and the Conspiratorial Enemy**

- **Schreber:** His persecutors were ambiguous—sometimes God himself, sometimes Dr. Flechsig (his psychiatrist), whose soul had become entangled with his. The enemy was both intimate and omnipotent.
- **Nazism:** The Jew was the all-explaining conspiratorial enemy—simply weak and powerful, capitalist and Bolshevik, corrupting from within and plotting from without. This “Jewish world conspiracy” explained every setback.

**Parallel:** The **construction of an omnipotent, all-responsible enemy**. This enemy is both everywhere and nowhere, justifying perpetual vigilance and unlimited violence.

---

### **Connecting to Hitler’s Biography: The Messianic Paranoia**

While we cannot diagnose Hitler at a distance, historians and psychologists (like **Erich Fromm** in _The Anatomy of Human Destructiveness_ or **Robert G. L. Waite**) have noted psychological patterns that resonate with Schreber’s themes:

1.  **Grandiosity and Mission:** Hitler saw himself as the destined savior of Germany, chosen by Providence. Like Schreber’s belief in his unique role, Hitler’s self-concept was messianic.
2.  **Body/Biology Obsession:** Hitler was hypochondriacal, obsessed with purity (vegetarian, anti-smoking), and framed politics as a biological struggle. Schreber’s obsession with bodily transformation finds its political analog in the Nazi focus on the racial body.
3.  **Paranoid Worldview:** Hitler’s worldview was fundamentally paranoid and Manichean—a cosmic battle between Aryan and Jew. This mirrors the structured paranoia of Schreber’s delusional system.
4.  **The Will to Annihilation:** Schreber’s psychosis involved the destruction of his old self to create a new one. Hitler’s ideology necessitated the destruction of the old world (and its “inferior” peoples) to usher in the Thousand-Year Reich.

---

### **Important Caveat: Not Equating, but Analyzing a Structure**

It’s crucial to emphasize:

- Schreber was a **victim of severe mental illness**, not an ideologue.
- The parallel is in the **structure of thought**, not the moral equivalence.
- The German sociologist **Elias Canetti** in _Crowds and Power_ and philosopher **Eric L. Santner** in _My Own Private Germany: Daniel Paul Schreber’s Secret History of Modernity_ have explored this deeply. Santner argues Schreber’s memoirs reveal the psychic pressures of modern authority—pressures that found monstrous political expression in Nazism.

### **Conclusion: The Psychotic Core of Totalitarianism?**

The parallels suggest that **Nazi totalitarianism operated, at a collective level, with a logic similar to a psychotic delusional system**. It created a closed reality, defined by a divine/cosmic mission, obsessed with purity and invasion, and requiring the violent transformation of humanity. Schreber’s tortured, solitary experience prefigured, in a pathological form, the collective pathology that would engulf a nation. His memoirs stand as an uncanny prophetic text, not of events, but of a terrifying **structure of mind** that can, under certain historical conditions, become a structure of state.

The “small invasions” are not perceived as social maneuvers but as **literal, metaphysical attacks on the fabric of reality.**

The “Jewish question” was not a social issue but an existential, cosmic threat to the Aryan _Volkskörper_ (national body). The imagined “surplus enjoyment” of the Jew was reframed as a literal poison, a parasite, a virus destroying Germany.

  - **Expulsion** (forced emigration)
  - **Invasion** (to take back the stolen _Lebensraum_ and dismantle “Judeo-Bolshevism”)

This is not just a theory about Nazism, but a warning about the potential endpoint of any politics that replaces symbolic law with the literalization of paranoid fantasy. It explains why the transition from prejudice to genocide is not a simple increase in hatred, but a catastrophic shift in the very structure of reality for the persecuting group.
