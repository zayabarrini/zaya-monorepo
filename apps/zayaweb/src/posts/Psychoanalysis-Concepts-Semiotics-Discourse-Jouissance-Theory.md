# More examples of sentences of the Analyst's discourse

In Lacan's **Analyst's Discourse**, the subject \(\$\) is placed in the position of truth, and the analyst operates from the position of **a**, the object cause of desire, producing a new **S2** (knowledge) for the analysand. This discourse is structured as: **a → $**

**S2 → S1**

Here are some examples of sentences that reflect this structure:

Interpretations that target the analysand’s divided subjectivity ($):

1. **"O que você quer dizer com isso?"**

2. **"Por que isso te incomoda tanto?"**

3. **"Você percebe como repete essa mesma estrutura?"**

4. **"Isso que você diz, não diz mais do que imagina?"**

5. **"O que essa lembrança te faz sentir agora?"**

   **Sentences that act as interventions, creating a shift in meaning**

6. **"Você sabia que Freud falava disso?"** (Bringing S2, a new knowledge, into play.)

7. **"Mas quem falou isso, você ou sua mãe?"**

8. **"Você já pensou que pode não ser apenas sobre ele, mas sobre você?"**

9. **"Esse erro que você cometeu agora… por que será?"**

10. **"Interessante como você sempre esquece essa parte da história."**

    **Questions that open the analysand’s desire**

11. **"Se isso não te faltasse, o que sobraria?"**

12. **"E se não houvesse mais ninguém para culpar?"**

13. **"Você quer mesmo saber ou prefere continuar sem saber?"**

14. **"O que aconteceria se você tivesse isso que deseja?"**

15. **"Se pudesse escolher, o que realmente escolheria?"**

    **Sentences that point to the symbolic inconsistency (S1 and S2 in conflict)**

16. **"Parece que há algo que você não quer saber sobre isso."**

17. **"Quem é que está falando agora?"**

18. **"O que você deixou de dizer?"**

19. **"E o que você ganha em manter isso assim?"**

20. **"Será que essa certeza que você tem não esconde outra dúvida?"**

These sentences exemplify how the **Analyst’s Discourse** disrupts the analysand’s imaginary and symbolic identifications, leading to new formations of knowledge (**S2**), often through paradox, contradiction, and unexpected shifts in meaning.

The **beggar's discourse** operates on a fundamental asymmetry: the beggar **asks anything from anyone** , without a pre-established symbolic contract. This discourse often **disrupts social norms** because it reveals **pure demand** without the usual exchanges of recognition, merit, or reciprocity.**Structure of the Beggar’s Discourse in Lacanian Terms**

| ----------- | --------------------- | -------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Agent (S1)  | The Beggar            | Pure Demand (Demanda Pura)             | The beggar exposes the dependency on the Other, placing themselves in a position of absolute lack.                               |
| Other (S2)  | The Passerby          | Master Signifier (S1 que não responde) | The passerby holds the power to give or refuse without being bound by obligation.                                                |

---

**Types of Beggar’s Speech & Their Functions**

1. **Direct Demand:** "Me dá um trocado?" (Give me some change?)

- **Pure Demand:** Confronts the Other with a direct request, exposing lack.

2. **Victim Position:** "Estou há dias sem comer." (I haven’t eaten in days.)

- **Appeal to Compassion:** Engages the Other’s guilt, attempting to elicit a response.

3. **Moral Reversal:** "Deus te abençoe." (May God bless you.)

- **Inversion of Power:** The beggar assumes a symbolic authority, as if giving something instead of taking.

4. **Provocative or Angry:** "Ninguém se importa comigo!" (Nobody cares about me!)

- **Challenge to the Social Order:** Reveals resentment and can trigger rejection.

5. **Silent Demand:** A gesture, extended hands, or just presence.

- **Unspoken Interpellation:** Leaves the Other to decide how to interpret and respond.

---

**Peculiarities of the Beggar’s Discourse**

- **Excess of Demand:** Unlike structured social requests (business, contracts), the beggar’s request is unbounded—he asks without offering.

- **Social Discomfort:** People avoid beggars because they expose a raw, **pre-symbolic** form of need.

- **Unstable Social Bond:** The beggar depends entirely on **contingency** ; each passerby can be a provider or an executioner.

---

**Comparison with Other Discourses**

| Beggar’s Discourse   | Immediate response     | Demand is exposed in its naked, unmediated form.      |

---

**Final Thoughts**

The beggar's discourse is **subversive** because it **does not respect the usual laws of exchange** . It brings to light the **bare structure of demand** and forces the Other to **either respond or reject it** —both of which are uncomfortable.

# Formal Essay

**The Beggar's Discourse: A Lacanian Analysis**

**Introduction** The discourse of the beggar is structured around an asymmetrical exchange: the beggar asks for something from anyone, without a pre-established symbolic contract. Unlike other social exchanges, where demand is mediated by institutions, merit, or reciprocity, the beggar's discourse **reveals demand in its purest form** . This raw exposure of lack confronts the Other with a choice: to give, refuse, or ignore.From a Lacanian perspective, this discourse occupies a singular position, as it operates outside the structured social bonds governed by the four fundamental discourses (Master, University, Hysteric, Analyst). Instead, the beggar's discourse oscillates between **the Real (absolute need), the Imaginary (guilt, pity, rejection), and the Symbolic (money, language, power relations).**

---

**1. The Structure of the Beggar's Discourse**

Using Lacan’s discourse theory, we can represent the beggar's discourse as follows:

| Position    | Element in the Discourse | Lacanian Concept                       | Explanation                                                                                                                                         |
| ----------- | ------------------------ | -------------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Agent (S1)  | The Beggar               | Pure Demand (Demanda Pura)             | The beggar embodies lack in its rawest form, exposing the dependency on the Other.                                                                  |
| Other (S2)  | The Passerby             | Master Signifier (S1 que não responde) | The passerby holds the power to give or refuse, yet often remains silent, refusing to acknowledge the demand.                                       |
| Product (a) | The Alms (or Refusal)    | Object a (Resto da Troca)              | Whether the passerby gives money, ignores, or responds aggressively, the interaction produces an effect on both sides.                              |
| Truth ($)   | Social Exclusion         | Divided Subject ($) in its Bare Form   | The beggar represents the subject in its most fragile, exposed state—outside the symbolic protections of work, contracts, and structured exchanges. |

This structure reveals how **the beggar’s discourse is disruptive** : it forces an encounter with the Real of need, which is generally disavowed in social life.

---

**2. Modes of Beggar’s Speech & Their Effects**

1. **Direct Demand:**

- "Me dá um trocado?" (Give me some change?)

- **Effect:** Puts the Other in an immediate position of power, demanding a response.

2. **Victim Position:**

- "Estou há dias sem comer." (I haven’t eaten in days.)

- **Effect:** Calls upon the passerby’s guilt, triggering either pity or avoidance.

3. **Moral Reversal:**

- "Deus te abençoe." (May God bless you.)

- **Effect:** The beggar takes a position of symbolic authority, offering a blessing instead of just demanding.

4. **Aggressive or Provocative Demand:**

- "Ninguém se importa comigo!" (Nobody cares about me!)

- **Effect:** This disrupts the passerby’s comfort, making rejection explicit rather than implicit.

5. **Silent Demand:**

- Just extending hands or standing in a visible place.

- **Effect:** Leaves interpretation entirely to the Other, generating unease.

  Each of these modes reveals a different **strategy of demand** , playing with guilt, authority, or silence to provoke a response.

---

**3. The Beggar’s Discourse in Relation to Other Discourses**

| Discourse            | Structure of Demand                          | Response Mechanism                                                               |
| -------------------- | -------------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Master’s Discourse   | Controlled demand through power and law      | Demand is structured within hierarchy and obligation.                            |
| University Discourse | Knowledge and expertise mediate demand       | Demand is filtered through institutions.                                         |
| Hysteric’s Discourse | Exaggerated demand that questions the Other  | Demand is used to provoke desire and expose contradictions.                      |
| Analyst’s Discourse  | Demand transformed into a question of desire | Demand is deconstructed to reveal unconscious structures.                        |
| Beggar’s Discourse   | Demand in its rawest form                    | Confronts the Other with immediate responsibility, provoking guilt or rejection. |

Unlike these structured discourses, the **beggar’s discourse lacks mediation** , making it both unbearable and profoundly revealing.

---

**4. The Beggar's Discourse and the Social Order** The presence of the beggar destabilizes social life because it **exposes the fundamental lie of reciprocity** :

- Most social interactions involve an implicit **exchange** (services, recognition, power).

- The beggar **asks without offering** , forcing the Other into a **decision outside structured obligations** .

  This makes the beggar's discourse **uncomfortable and often ignored** —society prefers beggars to be out of sight, either hidden in institutions or excluded altogether.

---

**5. The Beggar’s Discourse and the Fantasy of the Other** From a Lacanian perspective, the beggar **incarnates the Other’s lack** :

- The passerby sees their **own vulnerability** reflected in the beggar.

- The refusal to give is often a refusal to **recognize one's own lack** .

  This explains why **some respond with anger** —the beggar forces an unwanted confrontation with the Real.

---

**6. The Beggar's Discourse as a Form of Resistance** Paradoxically, the beggar’s discourse is also a form of **subversion** :

- It **refuses productivity** —the beggar does not enter the capitalist economy.

- It **resists symbolic integration** —the beggar does not conform to structured demands.

- It **forces recognition** —even being ignored is a kind of response.

  In this sense, the beggar’s discourse is **both abject and powerful** , exposing the **fragility of social bonds** and the **contingency of symbolic exchange** .

---

**7. Conclusion: The Unbearable Demand** The beggar’s discourse **reveals what social life tries to repress** :

- Demand without structure.

- Lack without mediation.

- The Other’s radical contingency.

  This is why **beggars are often treated with hostility** —they expose a truth about social bonds that most people prefer to ignore.Ultimately, the beggar’s discourse serves as a **limit case of demand** , revealing the **underlying structure of all social exchange** : the request for recognition, care, and response.

# Structure a whores' discourse, discurso da puta

**The Whore’s Discourse: A Lacanian Analysis**

**Introduction** The whore’s discourse (discurso da puta) operates within an economy of **desire, jouissance, and exchange** , exposing the contradictions of social and sexual structures. Unlike the beggar, who demands without offering, the whore’s discourse is structured around an **explicit transaction** —a direct exchange of pleasure for money or other forms of compensation.From a **Lacanian perspective** , this discourse is complex because it navigates both **phallic jouissance (structured within the symbolic order) and Other jouissance (excess, transgression, enjoyment beyond the law).** The whore embodies a paradox: she is at once desired and condemned, central to the economy of jouissance yet marginalized within the social order.

---

**1. The Structure of the Whore’s Discourse**

Using Lacan’s discourse theory, we can represent the whore’s discourse as follows:

| Position    | Element in the Discourse   | Lacanian Concept                            | Explanation                                                                                |
| ----------- | -------------------------- | ------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| Agent (S1)  | The Whore                  | Object of Desire & Lack ($)                 | The whore embodies both the object of male fantasy and the subject reduced to exchange.    |
| Other (S2)  | The Client                 | Master Signifier (S1 que compra)            | The client occupies the position of supposed power, yet is also driven by lack and demand. |
| Product (a) | The Sexual Service         | Object a (Causa do Desejo)                  | The encounter produces a temporary satisfaction but leaves structural lack untouched.      |
| Truth ($)   | The Stigma of Prostitution | The Split Subject ($) that Society Disavows | The whore’s position is both necessary and rejected, exposing the hypocrisy of desire.     |

This structure reveals how **the whore’s discourse is one of paradox and exclusion** : it exposes the transactional nature of all desire while being socially repressed.

---

**2. Modes of the Whore’s Speech & Their Effects**

1. **Direct Transaction:**

- "Cem reais, sem beijo na boca." (100 reais, no kissing.)

- **Effect:** Reduces the encounter to a clear economic exchange, avoiding affective entanglement.

2. **Seductive Reframing:**

- "Vem comigo, eu te faço esquecer de tudo." (Come with me, I’ll make you forget everything.)

- **Effect:** The whore positions herself as an agent of fantasy, masking the raw transaction.

3. **Demand for Respect:**

- "Sou profissional, não sou qualquer uma." (I’m a professional, I’m not just anyone.)

- **Effect:** Asserts a symbolic boundary between controlled jouissance and degradation.

4. **Moral Reversal:**

- "Homem casado adora uma puta." (Married men love whores.)

- **Effect:** Exposes the contradictions of bourgeois morality.

5. **Silent Exchange:**

- A glance, a gesture, an agreed-upon price.

- **Effect:** Keeps the interaction within the realm of the unspoken, reinforcing its taboo nature.

  Each of these modes **negotiates the tension between desire and social order** , revealing the contradictions at the heart of sexuality.

---

**3. The Whore’s Discourse in Relation to Other Discourses**

| Discourse            | Structure of Demand                                      | Response Mechanism                                                    |
| -------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Master’s Discourse   | The client demands satisfaction from the whore           | The whore fulfills the demand but maintains her own terms.            |
| University Discourse | Prostitution is analyzed as an economic and social issue | The whore becomes an object of study, rather than a speaking subject. |
| Hysteric’s Discourse | The whore questions the hypocrisy of desire              | She provokes society’s contradictions, exposing its repression.       |
| Analyst’s Discourse  | The whore’s position reveals the structure of fantasy    | Her role highlights the constructed nature of desire and lack.        |

Unlike these structured discourses, the whore’s discourse **exists in tension between visibility and exclusion** —she is both central to the sexual economy and pushed to the margins.

---

**4. The Whore’s Discourse and the Fantasy of the Other** The whore’s position in discourse is structured by **the fantasy of the Other** :

- She represents the **woman without lack** , the one who “enjoys” freely.

- She embodies **excess** , fulfilling desires that “respectable” women must refuse.

- At the same time, she is **stigmatized** , bearing the **social consequences of unregulated jouissance** .

  This paradox explains why the whore is both **desired and despised** —she exposes a fundamental truth about desire: **it is always mediated by fantasy and power.**

---

**5. The Whore’s Discourse as a Form of Resistance** Unlike the beggar, whose discourse is purely one of **lack** , the whore’s discourse is also a **performance of power** :

- She **controls the transaction** , setting boundaries and conditions.

- She **turns the client into the one who lacks** , forcing him to acknowledge his need.

- She **exposes the limits of bourgeois morality** , revealing the hypocrisy of monogamy, respectability, and sexual repression.

  In this sense, the whore’s discourse is **not simply submissive** —it is also a form of subversion, revealing the **truth of social desire.**

---

**6. The Whore’s Discourse and the Real of Sex** From a Lacanian perspective, prostitution confronts the **Real of sex** , which is unrepresentable in language:

- It **reduces sex to an exchange** , exposing the illusion of romantic love.

- It **challenges the idea of spontaneous desire** , showing how all sexuality is mediated by power and money.

- It **reveals the impossibility of full jouissance** , since no transaction can fully satisfy.

  This is why the whore’s discourse remains **so troubling to society** —it forces an encounter with the truth that **sex is never “pure” or “natural”** but always entangled in **language, power, and economy** .

---

**7. Conclusion: The Whore as a Figure of Truth**

The whore’s discourse exposes the fundamental structure of desire:

- **Desire is transactional.**

- **Jouissance is always limited.**

- **Social order depends on repressing its own contradictions.**

  While society **pretends that love and sex are separate from money and power** , the whore’s discourse **reveals that this separation is a fantasy** —one that hides the deeper structures of **desire, exchange, and exclusion** .Ultimately, the whore’s discourse serves as a **limit case of sexual demand** , exposing the **underlying economy of jouissance** that governs all social bonds.

- Maior liberdade, mas também maior incerteza.

- Possibilidade de inventar novas formas de laço, mas sem garantia de sua sustentação.

# Based on the beggers' discourse, discurso do mendigo

What are the possible consequences based on responses when one begs a friend, family member, father, mother, partner

The **beggar's discourse** is structured around an asymmetry: the one who begs places themselves in a **position of need** , while the Other is in the **position of power** to give or refuse. The response from the Other determines the possible consequences of the relationship and the beggar’s position in it.

**Possible Consequences Based on Responses**

**1. Begging from a Friend**

- **Response: Gives (with ease)** → Strengthens the bond, but risks making the beggar dependent or diminishing their autonomy.

- **Response: Gives (with reluctance)** → Creates guilt, resentment, or obligation. The friend might distance themselves.

- **Response: Refuses (with justification)** → The beggar may feel rejected but may also be forced to find another solution.

- **Response: Refuses (harshly)** → Friendship may weaken or break.

**2. Begging from a Family Member**

- **Response: Gives (as duty)** → Reinforces family obligation but may lead to resentment.

- **Response: Gives (but with expectations)** → Creates a **debt** that may later be demanded in return.

- **Response: Refuses (but offers another form of help)** → Redirects the relationship from dependence to empowerment.

- **Response: Refuses (without empathy)** → Can cause a fracture in family ties, perceived abandonment.

**3. Begging from a Father**

- **Response: Gives (as authority)** → The father retains power, the beggar remains a dependent child.

- **Response: Gives (but humiliates)** → Reinforces submission, making the beggar feel small and powerless.

- **Response: Refuses (to teach autonomy)** → A symbolic **Name-of-the-Father** function, pushing the beggar to find their own way.

- **Response: Refuses (with rejection)** → A wound to the subject’s symbolic structure, possibly leading to resentment or a search for another paternal figure.

**4. Begging from a Mother**

- **Response: Gives (with unconditional love)** → Reinforces the **mother-child bond** , but risks infantilization.

- **Response: Gives (but suffocates)** → The mother may use the act of giving to maintain control.

- **Response: Refuses (to force independence)** → Can create suffering but also push the beggar toward autonomy.

- **Response: Refuses (with rejection)** → Can lead to feelings of **abandonment, resentment, or even radical rupture** .

**5. Begging from a Partner**

- **Response: Gives (with affection)** → Strengthens the relationship but can create an **unbalanced dynamic** .

- **Response: Gives (but with resentment)** → May lead to passive-aggressive behavior, later conflicts.

- **Response: Refuses (coldly)** → Can create distance, resentment, or even the end of the relationship.

**Key Psychoanalytic Themes in Begging**

- **Humiliation vs. Power:** The beggar exposes their lack, while the Other holds the power of decision.

- **Desire and Demand:** Every demand is a **demand for love** , and the response reveals the nature of the bond.

**Market’s Discourse – A Lacanian Structure**

In Lacanian theory, discourse structures define how **social bonds** and **desire** circulate. The **Market’s Discourse** is structured around **exchange, value creation, and demand** . | Agent (S1 – Master Signifier)               | Other (S2 – Knowledge)                        |
| ------------------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------- |
| Product/Service as Object a (Value, Offer)  | Customer (Lack, Demand)                       |
| Subject ($ - Divided, Entrepreneur, Seller) | Surplus Jouissance (Profit, Market Expansion) |

- **Product/Service (Object a):** The object of desire that circulates in the market.

- **Customer’s Lack (S2):** The demand that drives consumption and need.

- **Seller/Entrepreneur ($):** The divided subject who oscillates between responding to demand and structuring new desires.

- **Surplus Jouissance (Profit):** The excess that allows reinvestment, innovation, and continued growth.

**Formula of the Market’s Discourse:**

**1. The Product/Service (Object a) is offered as a response to a Lack.**

- "You need X? I offer X."

| Focus on stability and profits | Focus on scalability and disruption    |
| Linear growth                  | Exponential growth                     |
| Customers seek the company     | The company seeks and shapes customers |

**4. Risks & Failures in the Startup’s Discourse**

- **Failure to create demand** → No market fit

- **Inability to sustain desire** → Weak retention

- **Lack of scalability** → Stagnation

- **Excessive surplus without reinvestment** → Unsustainable growth

A startup must navigate these by continuously **pivoting, testing, and learning** .
