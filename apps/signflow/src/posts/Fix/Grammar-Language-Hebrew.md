# Hebrew
What's the difference:
כ, ח,ך

In Hebrew, the letters **כ** , **ח** , and **ך** are distinct in both pronunciation and usage:

1. **כ (Kaf)** :

- **Pronunciation** : It has a hard "k" sound, like in "kite."

- **Usage** : This is the standard form of the letter, used at the beginning or middle of a word.

2. **ך (Final Kaf)** :

- **Pronunciation** : It has the same pronunciation as **כ** .

- **Usage** : This is the final form of the letter **כ** . It is only used at the end of a word.

3. **ח (Chet)** :

- **Pronunciation** : It has a guttural "kh" sound, similar to the "ch" in "Bach."

- **Usage** : This is a separate letter from **כ/ך** and is used in all positions within a word.

These differences are crucial in reading, writing, and pronouncing Hebrew words correctly.

# How to type vowels in phone keyboard

In Hebrew, vowels are represented by _nikkud_ (diacritical marks) rather than separate letters. On most phone keyboards, these marks are not directly visible, but you can type them by following these steps:

1. **Enable Hebrew Keyboard** : Make sure the Hebrew keyboard is enabled on your phone.

2. **Typing Nikkud** :

- On iPhones: Press and hold the letter you want to add a vowel to. This should bring up a list of nikkud options.

- On Android devices: After typing the letter, press and hold on the letter. A small menu should appear with nikkud options for that letter.

3. **Common Nikkud Examples** :

- **ֻ (Kubutz)** : For "u" sound, press and hold the letter, then select the dot arrangement.

- **ִ (Hiriq)** : For "i" sound, press and hold, then select the single dot underneath.

- **ֵ (Tzeirei)** : For "e" sound, choose the two dots side by side.

- **ַ (Patach)** : For "a" sound, select the short horizontal line underneath.

- **ֹ (Holam)** : For "o" sound, choose the dot above.

Using nikkud is less common in everyday Hebrew text (e.g., in texting or casual writing) but is important in more formal or educational contexts.

# Word order types in Hebrew

Hebrew typically follows a **Subject-Verb-Object (SVO)** word order, similar to English, but it can also exhibit some flexibility in sentence structure. Here are the main word order types in Hebrew:1. **Standard Word Order: SVO**

- **Subject-Verb-Object** is the most common sentence structure, especially in modern Hebrew.

- Example:

  - **אני קורא ספר** (_Ani koreh sefer_) – "I read a book."

  - Here, "אני" (I) is the subject, "קורא" (read) is the verb, and "ספר" (book) is the object.

2. **Verb-Subject-Object (VSO)**

- In Biblical Hebrew and some formal contexts, you might see **Verb-Subject-Object** order.

- Example:

  - **אמר משה דבר** (_Amar Moshe davar_) – "Moses said a word."

  - Here, "אמר" (said) is the verb, "משה" (Moses) is the subject, and "דבר" (word) is the object.

3. **Subject-Object-Verb (SOV)**

- Less common, but sometimes used for emphasis in certain phrases.

- Example:

  - **אני ספר קורא** (_Ani sefer koreh_) – "I am reading a book."

  - Here, "אני" (I) is the subject, "ספר" (book) is the object, and "קורא" (read) is the verb.

4. **Adjectives** : In Hebrew, adjectives generally come **after** the noun they describe.

- Example:

  - **ספר טוב** (_Sefer tov_) – "A good book."

  - Here, "ספר" (book) is the noun, and "טוב" (good) is the adjective.

5. **Prepositional Phrases** : Prepositions come **before** nouns, as in English.

- Example:
  - **על השולחן** (_Al ha-shulchan_) – "On the table."

While Hebrew primarily follows SVO, its flexible structure allows for variations based on emphasis or stylistic choices, especially in poetry, Biblical text, or formal speech.
