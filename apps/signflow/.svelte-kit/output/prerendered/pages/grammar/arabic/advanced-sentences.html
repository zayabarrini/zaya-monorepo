Arabic uses synthetic structures where English uses analytic ones. Complex English sentences are often expressed through different grammatical mechanisms in Arabic.

Complex Sentences with Subordinate Clauses

1
"Although he, who is usually so reserved, protested loudly, no one listened to him."
مع أن هو الذي عادةً ما يكون هادئًا جدًا احتجّ بصوت عالٍ، لم يسمع له أحد.
Translation: Although he, who is usually so reserved, protested loudly, no one listened to him.
Syntax Analysis:
Concessive Clause (مع أن)
Main Clause 1
Relative Clause (الذي)
Main Clause 2
Structure: [Concessive Conjunction + Main Clause₁ (with embedded Relative Clause)] + Main Clause₂
Morphological Breakdown:
احتجّ = احـ (Form VIII prefix) + تج (root: protest) + ّ (gemination for intensity)
لم يسمع = لم (negation) + يـ (prefix) + سمع (hear)
له = لـ (to) + ـه (him)
2
"Because the man whose car was stolen had no insurance, he had to pay everything himself."
لأن الرجل الذي سُرِقَت سيارته لم يكن لديه تأمين، هو كان مضطرًا أن يدفع كل شيء بنفسه.
Translation: Because the man whose car was stolen had no insurance, he had to pay everything himself.
Syntax Analysis:
Causal Clause (لأن)
Main Clause with Relative
Relative Clause (الذي)
Passive Verb
Compound Tense
Structure: Causal Clause → [Main Clause containing Relative Clause with Passive] → Result Clause with Compound Tense
Morphological Breakdown:
سُرِقَت = سرق (root: steal) + ـت (feminine passive) = "was stolen"
كان مضطرًا أن يدفع = كان (was) + مضطرًا (obliged) + أن (to) + يـدفع (pay)
بنفسه = بـ (with) + نفس (self) + ـه (his)

Adverbial Constructions

3
"Regardless of the storm, they sailed on."
رغم العاصفة، استمروا في الإبحار.
Translation: Regardless of the storm, they continued sailing.
Syntax Analysis:
Concessive Adverbial (رغم)
Main Clause
Adverbial of Manner
Structure: Concessive Phrase + Main Clause with Verbal Noun
Morphological Breakdown:
استمروا = اسـ (Form X prefix) + تمر (continue) + ـوا (they)
في الإبحار = في (in) + الإبحار (sailing - verbal noun)
4
"Remembering his words, she acted wisely."
عندما تذكرت كلامه، هي تصرّفت بحكمة.
Translation: When she remembered his words, she acted wisely.
Syntax Analysis:
Temporal Adverbial Clause
Main Clause
Adverbial of Manner
Structure: Temporal Clause (translated from English participle) + Main Clause
Morphological Breakdown:
تذكرت = تـ (Form V prefix) + ذكر (remember) + ـت (she)
تصرفت = تـ (Form V prefix) + صرف (act) + ـت (she)
كلامـه = كلام (words) + ـه (his)

Compound Tenses & Modal Constructions

5
"He will have had to go."
هو سيكون قد اضطر إلى الذهاب.
Translation: He will have been obliged to go.
Syntax Analysis:
Future Perfect Modal
Modal Verb + Verbal Noun
Structure: Future Perfect of Obligation + Verbal Noun Complement
Morphological Breakdown:
سيكون قد اضطر = سـ (future) + يكون (will be) + قد (already) + اضطر (was obliged)
إلى الذهاب = إلى (to) + الـذهاب (going - verbal noun)
6
"She might have wanted to come."
هي قد تكون أرادت المجيء.
Translation: She might have wanted to come.
Syntax Analysis:
Modal Perfect Construction
Verb Chain with Complement
Structure: Modal (قد) + Perfect of "to be" + Perfect of "to want" + Verbal Noun
Morphological Breakdown:
قد تكون أرادت = قد (might) + تـكون (she be) + أرادت (she wanted)
المجيء = الـ (the) + مجيء (coming - verbal noun)

Passive Constructions

7
"The house cannot be sold."
البيت لا يمكن بيعه.
Translation: The house cannot be sold.
Syntax Analysis:
Impersonal Passive
Passive Verbal Noun
Structure: Subject + Impersonal Passive Verb + Passive Verbal Noun with Attached Pronoun
Morphological Breakdown:
لا يـمكن = لا (not) + يـ (it) + مكن (is possible) = "it is not possible"
بيعه = بيع (selling) + ـه (its) = "its selling"
8
"Attempts were made to help him."
جُرِّبَ محاولة مساعدته.
Translation: An attempt to help him was tried.
Syntax Analysis:
Passive Main Verb
Verbal Noun + Complement
Subordinate Verbal Phrase
Structure: Passive Verb + Verbal Noun with Embedded Purpose Clause
Morphological Breakdown:
جُرِّبَ = جرب (try) + passive form = "was tried"
محاولة مساعدته = محاولة (attempt) + مساعدة (helping) + ـه (him)

Reported Speech & Complex Relative Clauses

9
"He claimed he was innocent."
هو ادّعى أنه بريء.
Translation: He claimed that he was innocent.
Syntax Analysis:
Main Reporting Clause
Reported Speech Clause
Complementizer (أن)
Structure: Reporting Verb + أن (that) + Reported Content Clause
Morphological Breakdown:
ادّعى = ادّعـ (Form VIII prefix) + ع (claim) = "claimed"
أنه = أن (that) + ـه (he)
10
"The question, which has been unresolved and discussed again and again for years, is dividing society."
السؤال الذي كان غير محلول و يُناقش مرارًا وتكرارًا لسنوات، يُقسِّم المجتمع.
Translation: The question, which has been unresolved and discussed again and again for years, is dividing society.
Syntax Analysis:
Main Clause
Extended Relative Clause
Perfect Passive
Present Passive
Multiple Adverbials
Structure: Subject + [Long Relative Clause with Multiple Verbs & Adverbials] + Main Verb + Object
Morphological Breakdown:
كان غير محلول = كان (was) + غير (un-) + محلول (solved)
يُناقش = يـ (passive prefix) + ناقش (discuss) = "is discussed"
يُقسِّم = يـ (he/it) + قسّم (divide) = "divides"

Key Observations about Arabic Complex Syntax

  • Subordinate Clauses in Arabic are often introduced by specific particles (أن، الذي، لأن، مع أن) rather than through participial constructions as in English.
  • English passive constructions are often rendered using Arabic's synthetic passive verb forms (يُفعل، فُعل) or impersonal constructions (لا يمكن).
  • Compound tenses in English (will have had to) are expressed through Arabic's auxiliary verbs (كان، سيكون) combined with participles or verbal nouns.
  • Long relative clauses in English are usually rendered similarly in Arabic using الذي/التي, but Arabic allows more flexibility in clause ordering.
  • English gerunds and participles ("remembering," "regardless of") are typically translated using Arabic verbal nouns (المصادر) or adverbial phrases.
  • Reported speech in Arabic requires the complementizer أن (that) more consistently than in English.
  • Multiple adverbials in English sentences are handled through prepositional phrases and adverbs in Arabic, often with different ordering.
  • English modal perfect constructions ("might have wanted") are expressed through combinations of قد (might) with perfect or compound tense forms.
  • Parenthetical expressions in English ("who is usually so reserved") are typically integrated as relative clauses in Arabic without parentheses.
  • Arabic tends toward synthetic expression where English uses analytic constructions, resulting in different syntactic packaging of the same semantic content.
