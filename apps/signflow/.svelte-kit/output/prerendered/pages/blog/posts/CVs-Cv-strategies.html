When considering issues of time commitment, compensation, equity, and other contributions, everything…

When considering issues of time commitment, compensation, equity, and other contributions, everything needs to be carefully and honestly customized for each member of the founding team. Everyone may have different goals—that’s fine—but differences need to be brought to the table and discussed fully and openly. Once the business is underway, if team members are surprised to find out that one person is working on the new business 24/7 while another only works 4 hours a day, the team will likely break down into arguments and recriminations. So startup founders need to work hard to be honest with each other about exactly what they’re willing to compromise on.

What other resources will each team member contribute? These resources may include specific talents and skills; individually owned pieces of intellectual property (such as product designs, software code, written documents, patents, copyrights, trademarks, and so on); personal contacts and networking skills; access to physical assets (such as workspace, machinery or tools); and financial resources.

How will the decisions that affect the business be made? Ultimately, even in equal partnerships, one member of the founding team must have the final decision-making authority, otherwise the company risks getting stuck in indecision… and that’s the only thing who can’t afford it. So which member of the founding team will be the entrepreneurial CEO?

What personal goals does each member of the founding team bring to the business? How many months or years does each member expect to remain engaged in the business? How do the team members’ personal life goals fit into the business’s growth expectations?

Be prepared to lose friendships. Starting a business is one of the most emotionally draining activity…

Be prepared to lose friendships. Starting a business is one of the most emotionally draining activities you can engage in, right up there with marriage and parenthood. Given the odds that your new startup will fail (most do), try to imagine whether your friendship would survive a bankruptcy. It’s not necessarily essential that it will (we’re not talking about starting a business with your spouse), but understand that failure is a real possibility. -

Coding, design, production, sales, finance, operations, marketing, and the like are execution skills—and without good execution, it will be difficult to succeed. -

It’s a combination of vision, passion, leadership, commitment, communication skills, hypomania, financing ability, and, above all, risk-taking that brings the above pieces together and creates a company that plays a value-producing role in our economy.

A successful company requires all the above pieces, to realize that any functional skill set an e…

A successful company requires all the above pieces, to realize that any functional skill set an entrepreneur starts with can be augmented with the others, and to understand that most of the rewards (after adjusting for the cost of capital) accrue to the entrepreneurial role, as they have for hundreds of years. -

You realize that it’s much more fun when you don’t have money and that money can be the worst thing you could ever have as a personal goal. If you’re lucky enough to genuinely feel this way, it’s a surreal feeling that’s the closest thing to peace because you realize that it’s the challenges and the work that you love. Your currencies are freedom, autonomy, responsibility and recognition. These are the same currencies as the people you want around you. -

You feel like a father to your clients because they’ll never realize how much you love them, and they’re the ones who validate that you’re not crazy. You want to embrace each one of them. They mean the world to you.
